# Note 08 — *The Girl Who Made Me Believe in People*

**To:** Malar — the one who restored something in me I didn't even know was damaged.
**From:** Hirthik Balaji
**Subject:** How you rebuilt my faith in people — quietly, without a plan, just by being exactly who you are.

---

> *"Ni hapi ah comfy ah irukanum — avlothan."*
> — Me, at some point, trying to put the whole thing into words. But you had been showing me this for years already.

Let me tell you something about what happens to you when the people around you let you down.

It doesn't always look dramatic. It's not always a single betrayal that cracks everything open. Sometimes it's gradual — the accumulation of small disappointments, of people who said they'd be there and weren't, of friendships that turned out to be more strategic than they appeared, of care that came with conditions attached. Sometimes it's just the slow build of understanding that the world is not always as warm as you hoped, and the quiet, exhausting decision to manage your expectations of people before they manage you.

I had some of that in me, Malar. More than I probably knew. A certain guardedness that had settled in without announcement. A reflex toward self-sufficiency that was only partly about strength and partly about not wanting to be disappointed again.

And then there was you. And you took that whole careful architecture apart, not by arguing with it but by *demonstrating* that it was wrong.

---

## Part One: *The Specific Ways People Fail Each Other*

I want to be honest here because this note deserves honesty at its foundation.

Friends cancel. That's the mildest version. Friends say they'll show up and find reasons not to. Friends listen to your problems with one eye on the clock. Friends give advice that serves their need to feel useful more than your need to be helped. Friends compare their hardship to yours in a way that subtly competes rather than connects. Friends are present when you're fun and absent when you're struggling. Friends say *always* and mean *for now*.

None of this is malicious. Most of it is just human limitation — people are busy, people are scared, people have their own pain competing for their attention. I understand it. But understanding why it happens doesn't fully remove the effect of *experiencing* it. And the effect, over time, is a kind of low-level distrust. A learned hesitation to fully count on anyone.

I had some of that. Not a lot. But some.

And you undid it.

---

## Part Two: *What You Did Differently*

You were consistent when people aren't usually consistent.

Here is the thing about consistency: it is the hardest form of love to sustain and therefore the most convincing form of love to receive. Anyone can be extraordinary once. Anyone can show up for the big moments — the crises, the celebrations, the times when showing up is clearly the right thing to do and the story demands it. The real test is the *ordinary* moments. The Tuesday afternoon. The unremarkable day. The "nothing significant is happening but I'm thinking of you" moment.

You passed that test, repeatedly, over years.

You weren't there just for the dramatic chapters of my life. You were there for the uneventful ones. The routine ones. The ones that barely register as chapters at all — just the normal flow of days where we talked about food and films and school and friends and the small absurd things that fill ordinary conversations. You showed up for those too, with the same presence, the same warmth, the same genuine interest in my actual life.

Do you understand what that does to a person? Not just intellectually — what it *does*, in the body, in the place where trust lives? It rewrites the story. It says: *some people actually do stay. Some people actually do mean it.* And I don't have to qualify every relationship with "for now" because I have evidence that "always" is a real category.

**You are my evidence that always is a real category.**

---

## Part Three: *The Thing About Your Honesty*

One of the things that rebuilt my faith in people most specifically was this: you were honest with me even when it would have been easier not to be.

This matters because dishonesty — even well-intentioned, gentle dishonesty — is a form of distance. When someone tells you what you want to hear instead of what they actually think, they are not fully *with* you. They are managing you. And over time, even if the individual deceptions are small, the cumulative effect is that you can't quite trust what you're receiving. You don't know where the performance ends and the real person begins.

You never gave me that uncertainty. What you said was what you thought. What you felt was what you expressed. What you praised was genuinely praiseworthy in your eyes, and what you pushed back on was something you actually disagreed with. There was no management, no calculated presentation, no version of yourself shaped for my consumption.

There was just you, actually.

The relief of that — the *rest* of it — I want to try to describe. It is the feeling of being able to take off the armour that you didn't realise you were wearing. It is the specific relaxation of a muscle that had been tense so long you'd stopped noticing the tension. It is the discovery that not all connection requires vigilance — that some people are exactly what they appear to be and you can just... *be*, around them. Fully, uncomplicatedly be.

That is not common. That is not something I encounter everywhere. But I found it with you, and because I found it with you, I know it exists — and that knowing makes the whole human project feel more survivable.

---

## Part Four: *The Specific Kindness That Got Through*

There are acts of kindness that slide past your defences because they're so *specific* they can't be generic.

Generic kindness is easy to receive with a certain detachment. *"Let me know if you need anything."* *"I'm here if you want to talk."* These are kind things to say and I don't diminish them. But they don't necessarily touch the guarded place because they could be said to anyone.

Your kindness was never generic. It was *specific*. It was aimed at *me*, at the particular version of me in that particular moment. When you noticed I hadn't eaten and mentioned it — that was a detail. You had to be paying attention to have that detail. When you remembered something I mentioned weeks ago and referenced it when it became relevant again — that required attention and retention and the decision to file it away because it mattered. When you picked up on a tone in my messages and asked *"enna achu?"* — that required you to actually be looking.

You paid attention to me, Malar. And being paid that quality of attention — especially by someone who had no obligation to, who chose it freely — did something to the part of me that had started to believe it was asking too much to be truly seen.

It said: *you're worth the attention.* And then it said: *and so are other people. And so is the world.*

That restoration of faith — not just in you but in *people*, in what's possible between people — is one of the most significant things you've ever done for me. And you did it just by being yourself.

---

## Part Five: *Why This Matters Beyond Just Us*

Here is the thing about having someone restore your faith in people: it doesn't stay contained to your relationship with them. It *radiates*.

Because of you, I am more willing to trust. Not recklessly — I'm not talking about naivety. But more *willing*. More open to the possibility that the person in front of me might also be genuine. More inclined to give the benefit of the doubt. More likely to stay in a relationship through difficulty rather than retreating at the first sign of complexity.

Because of you, I extend more grace. I am more patient with people's limitations because you showed me that the right person is patient with yours. I am more willing to let things be imperfect because you showed me that imperfect and real is worth more than polished and distant.

Because of you, I try to be more present. Because I know from your example what it feels like to have someone actually *there* — and knowing what that feels like makes me want to be that for other people.

You made me better at being in the world with other people. Not by lecturing me or instructing me or holding me to standards, but by modelling, quietly and completely, what it looks like when a person chooses to show up fully.

**That is an extraordinary gift to give someone. You gave it to me, and through me, it has touched every relationship I've had since.**

---

## Epilogue: *What I Believe Now*

Because of you, Malar, I believe the following things with much more conviction than I held them before:

I believe that *always* is achievable. That people can mean it and keep meaning it. That consistency is possible.

I believe that honesty and kindness are not in competition. That you can tell the truth and be gentle. That real care means real seeing.

I believe that specificity is love. That paying attention to someone's particular details is one of the most intimate things you can do.

I believe that people who are exactly what they appear to be exist — and that finding them is not luck, it is the reward for being the kind of person they would choose.

I believe that being cared for well makes you better at caring for others. That love doesn't deplete — it teaches.

All of that — every single one of those beliefs — has your fingerprints on it.

**You made me a person who believes in people. That might be the greatest thing you've ever done for me.**

And I am not done being grateful.

---

*— Hirthik Balaji, a believer, because of you.*
