# NOTE 2
## The train station
Malar, do you know how much I planned that day? You were leaving on your train, and I wasn't even supposed to be there. You hadn't asked me to come. Nobody expected me to come.

But I planned the whole thing. Bike from home at 10:20. Reached the station. Sat on the bench at the 3rd platform — the one facing your side — and waited. Just to see you for sixty seconds. Maybe less.

When I saw you step off the coach with your bag, I turned away because I didn't want you to catch me before I was ready. I was actually nervous. Like what if you thought it was too much?

And then you saw me. And you said later — "theriyum ni varuva nu athan pachaiyaa na varala" — you knew I'd come, and that's exactly why you didn't wear the green dupatta. You little devil.

I drove back alone that day smiling to myself like a complete fool. And I'd do it again. A hundred times.

## The 3 am messages I saved for you
There were so many nights I'd be lying awake — no real reason, just that heavy quiet — and I'd think about you. Just like that. Out of nowhere.

One night, at around 1 am, I was listening to an RJB podcast when the thought just landed: I miss her. Right now. This moment. So I opened a note on my phone and started writing — everything I felt, everything I wanted to say but couldn't text yet. Page after page.

And then I started sending them to you. Not because I wanted anything back. Just because I thought — if I ever disappear from your life, if you ever forget that someone truly, completely rooted for you — you'll have these. You can read them on a bad day and know that once upon a time, someone wrote pages about you at 1 am because you were worth every word.

I titled the folder in Google Drive. I still have it. I never deleted it.

## The night you were hurting and I couldn't leave
You told me you were going through something with Niveda. You didn't give me all the details right away. But I could feel it in every one-word reply, in the dry "hmm"s, in the long gaps between messages.

So I didn't leave. I just kept talking. I asked you what you'd say to her if you could. I told you to let it out — all of it. I stayed up past 2 am, typing and retyping because I didn't want to say the wrong thing. I wanted every word to land right.

And then at some point you said: "see enta pesura orea jeevan um enala sogama la iruka kudathu." That the only person chatting with you shouldn't be sad because of you. You were hurting and you were still worrying about me.

That broke me a little. In the best way. Because I realized — this is who she is. Even in her worst moments, she thinks of others first.

I told you to bedsheet la moodu, pillow la muji pothu azhuthuru. And I meant it — let it out, I'll wait.

## When you were sick and I became your doctor
You told me casually — stomach pain on the bus, hospital visit, scan done. You said it like it was nothing. But I panicked quietly while keeping a calm face for you.

I researched ovarian cysts at midnight. I read about what to eat, what to avoid, what exercises were safe and which ones weren't. I sent you notes. I told you about hot bag therapy — socks la rice potu warm pannitu valikura place la vai, it helps.

When you said the pain was worse on the bus, I thought about you the whole day. When you said you couldn't even take painkillers properly, I wanted to reach through the phone and just sit with you.

And when the doctor's name turned out to be Nivetha — and you texted me that with a laughing emoji — I swear that's the moment I knew: even in the middle of pain, you could still find something to smile about. That's rare, Malar. That's you.

I kept checking in. Every few hours. Because you matter more than you let yourself believe.

## The Friendship Day letter
I had been writing it for weeks without realizing it. Every conversation, every memory, every late night — it was all quietly becoming a letter. And on Friendship Day, I just sent it.

I told you about the pizza parties at Bocz. About how your infectious smile brightened even my gloomiest days. About the late-night adventures around Kalaivasal. I told you about the first time I saw your name on that chain — Malar — and how something in me pulled toward you from that moment.

I told you that you were more than a friend. That you were someone who made me feel seen and heard. That God must have known I'd need a person exactly like you.

And your reply — "Woahhhh I'm stunned... really unexpected... thank you so much" — I read that message four times. I kept going back to it. Because for once, I had managed to say something true and have it land exactly right.

You said your first Friendship Day wish that year went to me. I'm still not over that.

## The NATA exam morning
The morning before your NATA exam, I sent you a message before you even woke up. I wanted it to be the first thing you read.

"Seri malareey class attend pannu nee concentrate pani mass paaniru presentation ella... jus rock — unnala mudiyu eppadiyu mass panniruva irunthalum solren... all the best heey... heartfelt-advance."

I know it sounds simple. But I meant every syllable of it. I wanted you to walk into that exam hall knowing someone believed in you — not just in your talent, but in you. In the version of you that shows up even when she's exhausted, even when the hostel food is terrible, even when everything feels uncertain.

And when you came back and told me you wrote well — that you were satisfied — I felt prouder than I would have felt about any exam of my own.

That's what you do to people, Malar. You make their wins feel personal.

## The birthday note I kept in my phone
Your birthday is September 20th. I start thinking about it weeks before. Not what to gift — I'd think about what to say. Because with you, words always felt more important than things.

I had a note in my phone — a proper, handwritten kind of note in digital form — that I'd been adding to for weeks. Lines I wanted to say. Memories I didn't want to forget. Things I noticed about you that I was scared I'd never get to tell you.

When you finally read it, you said: "Achoooo semaaaaaaaaaaaa no wordsss uh po... kalaila padicha muthal msg wahh spr uh..! Very happiiiiiii to read these."

That "no words" — that's the one. That's what I wrote it for. Not for the thank you. For that one moment where you felt something so real you ran out of words.

You deserved to know how extraordinary you are. I just tried to say it in a way that didn't let you brush it off.
