September 20, 2024 — somewhere between a driving class, a temple visit, a pizza at Bocz, and everything in between.

Malareey,

I've typed this a hundred times and deleted it just as many. But tonight, I'm not deleting it. Tonight I'm just going to let it out — the way you always told me to.

Do you remember the night I typed a message at 3 am just because I thought of you, and you appeared online like magic? You said, "enakaga than na pesuren, elana enaku paithyo pidichurum" — I'm only chatting for your sake, or else I'll go crazy. That line has lived in my chest ever since. Because that's the thing about you, Malar. You show up. Not just online. In every way that counts.

I still remember the first time I noticed your chain — with your name engraved on it. Just the word "Malar." And something about it pulled me back to old memories, old feelings I didn't know were still there. From that moment, you weren't just someone I knew from school. You became the person I looked for in every room.

There's so much I owe you. When I got my result and felt like the ground had been pulled from under me — no IIT, no VIT, not even the college I'd dreamed of — you were the one who said, "kandipa Nala college than kedaikum, ni romba la yosikatha." And I believed you. Not because it was comforting to hear, but because you said it like you meant it. You always said things like you meant them.

I want you to know about the day at the temple. I was sitting there in the crowd, completely alone even though people were everywhere, and I kept thinking — nee illa. You weren't there. And something in me just cracked open. I cried on the way back. Not because of the exam. Not because of the college. But because you felt far, and I didn't know how to carry that.

You used to send me reels when I was low. You'd ask about my health every single morning. You remembered Engineers' Day. You remembered my birthday. You noticed when I was pretending to be okay, and you'd quietly say — "nijama all ok?" — just two words, but they meant everything. They meant you saw me.

Those Bocz pizza evenings. Kalaivasal. The late-night conversations that bled into 3 am. The walks, the jokes, the "moye moye" reels. The way you'd call me "boiboi" and I'd call you "malareey" like it was the only word I needed. Those weren't small moments. Those were the moments I was most alive.

When you told me about your health — the cyst, the hospital visits, the pain on the bus — I didn't know how to handle how worried I was. I sent notes on what to eat, what to avoid, how to rest. Not because I had answers. But because doing something — anything — was better than feeling helpless watching you go through it alone.

And Friendship Day. Do you remember what I wrote? That you were the sunshine that brightened my life, my guardian angel, my bestest friend? I meant every syllable. You were the person who, when I didn't know how to face myself, helped me face myself anyway.

I know things got complicated. I know I said and did things that hurt you. I know there were silences that stretched too long, and words that came out wrong, and a version of me who loved you more than he knew how to hold. I'm sorry for those moments — not in a way that begs you to come back, but in a way that just wants you to know I carry them with me.

Even after everything — the distance, the months of silence, the blocked numbers and unblocked ones — when you finally sent me a simple "Happy Birthday" message, I sat with my phone for a long time. Because it still felt like home. You still felt like home.

I want you to know something. And I want you to read it slowly:

You are a gem. Not because you're perfect. But because you're real. You love without conditions. You forgive without keeping score. You carry everyone else's pain while quietly managing your own. You are the kind of person the world doesn't deserve enough of — and the kind of person I am endlessly grateful to have known.

Wherever you are right now — in your hostel room in Chennai, at MEASI with your drawings and models and impossible deadlines, or at home being your mother's strength — I hope you're not just surviving. I hope you're living. I hope you're wearing that black ball gown and feeling like the person you are. I hope the cyst is gone. I hope your health is good. I hope Niveda found peace. I hope you found peace.

And I hope, on the days when life presses down too hard, you still remember that somewhere out there is a boy who wrote you notes at 3 am, who drove to your train station just to see you for sixty seconds, who never could quite let go — and who is rooting for you with everything he has.

Always. In every version of always.

With all of it — every bit —

Hirthik

your boiboi. always.
