# Note 12 — *Dear Malar: The Letter I Should Have Written Sooner*

**To:** Malar — in the direct voice this should always have had.
**From:** Hirthik Balaji
**Subject:** Everything I want to say, plainly, finally, without preamble.

---

*Dear Malar,*

I have written you eleven notes now — elaborate, structured, themed — and in each one I have tried to approach you from a particular angle, to make the case for who you are from a specific direction. And all of that has been true. All of it has meant every word.

But I want to write this last one differently. Not as an argument or an essay. Not as a list or an analysis. Just as a letter. The kind you write to a person when you want them to simply *hear you* — without architecture, without argument, without the careful construction of a thesis. Just words, directly from me to you, about what I want you to know.

So. Dear Malar.

---

Here is what I want you to know.

I want you to know that the day you first messaged me — that casual, ordinary *"Hello sir"* — I had no idea what was beginning. I could not have predicted you. The specific shape of who you turned out to be, the particular quality of your presence in my life, the degree to which you would matter — none of it was visible in that first message. Which is the beautiful, slightly terrifying thing about beginnings. They don't announce what they're starting.

You started everything.

I want you to know that our conversations are some of the most valuable things I have. I have friends, and I have family, and I have people I love — but what you and I built in those thousands and thousands of messages is its own specific, irreplaceable thing. A world with its own language and weather. A space where I could be completely unguarded and always receive something genuine in return. I know how rare that is. I know it is not something you find everywhere or with everyone. I want you to know that I know.

I want you to know that your honesty has made me better. Every time you told me the truth when I wanted to hear something comfortable, you were doing something for me that required courage and care in equal measure. You could have let me be wrong. You could have agreed and moved on. You chose differently, consistently, because you cared about the actual outcome — about me actually being okay, actually growing, actually becoming someone more whole. That choice changed me. More than once. In directions I am still discovering.

I want you to know that your love story — the one with Arvind, the one you told me about so honestly and so fully — has never left me. Not because of the heartbreak, but because of how you carried it. The dignity. The refusal to make him into a villain even when you had every reason. The choice to prioritise your own peace over the easier option of bitterness. You were nineteen years old, or something close to it, and you were making choices that most adults never manage to make. I have thought about that more times than I can count. It is one of the things I point to when I need a model for how to be.

I want you to know that the way you take care of people — the checking in, the food questions, the good mornings, the prayers, the noticing — has shaped how I try to take care of people. You didn't give me a lesson. You gave me a model. And the model is running in me now, informing how I show up, reminding me what it looks like when someone actually *means it*. I am a better friend because of you. Not because you told me how, but because you showed me.

I want you to know that your strength is not invisible to me. I know you probably don't narrate your own story as a story of strength — you're not that kind of person, you don't perform your resilience or require it to be acknowledged. But I see it. The girl who got up on the mornings that were hard and kept going without making a production of having kept going. The girl who survived something genuinely painful and came out the other side *warmer*, not colder. The girl who carries her own weight and still has energy left to carry some of mine. That is strength. Real, quiet, extraordinary strength. And you should know that someone sees it.

I want you to know that our laughter matters to me as much as our depth. I don't want this to be a collection of notes that only acknowledges the serious things, because the serious things were never the whole of us. The silly things were equal parts of us — the film debates, the roasts, the specific brands of absurdity that only landed because we both understood the exact reference. Those moments were not decoration. They were *life*. They were the texture of actually knowing each other, actually being *in* each other's day, actually sharing the experience of being alive in this specific time and place and culture. I cherish the laughter as much as I cherish the depth.

I want you to know that I am proud of you. I don't say that in a condescending way — I'm not congratulating you from above. I mean it as someone who has been watching you navigate your life and who has seen, up close, what that navigation has required of you. I am proud of how you've handled the hard things. I am proud of who you are choosing to be. I am proud that you are someone who has kept her goodness intact through the things that could have corrupted it. That is worth being proud of.

I want you to know that you deserve everything you have ever given. This sounds simple but I mean it very specifically. The quality of care you pour out so freely — the attentiveness, the honesty, the unconditional showing up — you deserve to receive all of that back. You deserve to be seen, specifically and lovingly, by everyone who gets to be in your life. You deserve the good morning message that actually means something. You deserve the friend who tells you the truth. You deserve the person who notices when you're off and asks. You deserve the kind of love you give — and I mean that as a description of what should exist in your life, not as a suggestion that it doesn't exist yet. I'm just saying: *you are worth all of it, and more, and don't ever settle for less.*

I want you to know that I will keep showing up. Not because I owe you anything — but because who you are makes showing up something I want to do. Because this friendship has been one of the genuine goods of my life. Because the world with you in it is more worth being in, and the version of me that knows you is the version I would choose.

I want you to know — finally, plainly, without needing it to be bigger than it is — that I am grateful. Not in a moment of particular feeling, not in a surge of emotion that will fade. Just: in my ordinary, steady, daily state of existence, when I think of the things that have made my life good, you are there. You are a constant in that accounting. You are one of the reasons the ledger tilts the way it does.

**Thank you, Malar. Thank you for the hello. Thank you for every message after. Thank you for who you are and how you've been it and who I've become because of it.**

Thank you for being the main character you've always been — in your own story, and in mine.

---

*I don't know exactly what the next chapters look like. But I know they're better because you're in them.*

*With full heart and no performance —*

*Hirthik Balaji.*
