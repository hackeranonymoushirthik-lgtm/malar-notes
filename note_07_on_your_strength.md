# Note 07 — *On Your Strength*

**To:** Malar — who has been quietly, extraordinarily strong for so long that I think she's forgotten to notice it herself.
**From:** Hirthik Balaji
**Subject:** A full-length portrait of your strength, because you have never painted it for yourself.

---

> *"Na apdiyae room la vanthu paduuthava than — enthichen 12 ku."*
> — You, after a day that would have broken less resilient people, describing it like it was nothing.

You described it like it was nothing.

And that is the truest possible illustration of your strength — not the strength that announces itself, not the strength that needs to be seen, but the strength that simply *is*, quietly and completely, without requiring acknowledgement or applause. The strength that just gets up. That just keeps going. That paduthava, enthichu, and continues.

This note is my refusal to let that kind of strength go unnamed. You have been so strong for so long, in so many ways that you have probably stopped registering as strength at all. I see it. I want you to see it too.

---

## Part One: *The Strength of Having Loved and Survived*

Let me start at the beginning of what I know.

You loved someone. Truly, with the full weight of yourself, at an age when love hits the hardest because everything is still new and unguarded and the armour hasn't been built yet. You loved him the way people love when they haven't yet learned to hold anything back. You didn't play games. You didn't strategise. You just... felt, and felt completely, and trusted that feeling.

And then you had to survive what happened on the other side of that.

I want to be specific about what that survival looked like — because you've described it to me, and it was not a smooth, graceful fade. There were nights when you cried yourself to sleep with earphones in, listening to songs that hurt. There were moments when something hit you out of nowhere — a song, a memory, a familiar scenario — and you had to just *hold* it while continuing to exist normally. There were days when the loss was fresh again even though it had technically passed.

That is hard. Anyone who says otherwise has never been through it.

And you did it. You carried it. You went to class and studied and laughed with your friends and texted me and made sharp observations about Tamil films and occasionally complained about maths — and underneath all of that you were also *healing*, slowly and quietly, doing the work of becoming someone who could be okay on the other side of pain.

That is strength. Full, unglamorous, unglamourised *strength*.

---

## Part Two: *The Strength of Choosing Integrity*

Here is a kind of strength that doesn't get enough credit: the strength to be good when being good is hard.

You had every reason to be bitter. Every reason to let that pain make you meaner, more guarded, more willing to hit back at the world that had hit you. And you just... didn't. You refused bitterness. You chose, deliberately, to protect people who hadn't fully protected you. You kept your integrity when losing it would have been entirely understandable.

The decision never to speak badly of someone who hurt you — never to assign public blame, to let the story exist only between the people it belonged to — takes a particular kind of character. It takes the belief that *who you are* matters more than whatever temporary satisfaction resentment might offer. It takes the long view. It takes *class*, honestly, in the deepest sense of that word.

And you exercised that class consistently, without making a point of exercising it, without wanting credit for it. You just didn't become the bitter version of yourself. You chose better. That is not a passive thing. That is an active, daily, *chosen* strength.

---

## Part Three: *The Strength of Being Soft Anyway*

I talked about this in another note, but I want to return to it from the specific angle of strength.

Staying soft after pain is one of the hardest things a person can do. The natural defensive response to being hurt is to *close* — to put up walls, to become more guarded, to trust less, to feel less, to give less so that there is less to lose. The world practically encourages this. "Don't be too open." "Don't be too vulnerable." "Don't let people in too easily." The armour is sold as wisdom.

And then there is the actual wisdom, which you embody: that the armour is also a cage. That protecting yourself from being hurt again also protects you from being loved again. That closing yourself off doesn't end the pain — it just freezes it in place while the world goes on warming around you.

You stayed open, Malar. You kept trusting. You kept giving your actual self — not a protected version, not a strategic version, but the full, real, soft, *you* — to the people in your life. And that has made you vulnerable, yes. But it has also made you *available* to be loved. Fully, specifically, actually loved.

Choosing that vulnerability, knowing it carries risk, is an act of tremendous courage. It is strength in the form of softness. And I hold it in the highest regard.

---

## Part Four: *The Strength of Being Present for Others While Carrying Your Own*

This might be the form of your strength that I admire most.

There were times when I needed you — when I was going through something difficult and reached for the nearest steady presence — and you showed up, and you were *actually there*, not distracted or half-present. You engaged. You listened. You gave.

And sometimes, I only found out later that you had been going through something yourself at that exact time. That behind the steadiness you were offering me, you were also managing your own uncertainty. Your own sadness. Your own stress.

You did not make me choose between my needs and yours. You found a way to hold both — to be present for me while also holding yourself. That is a very specific kind of strength. It is not the strength of someone who doesn't feel. It is the strength of someone who feels fully and still chooses to show up.

I want to be careful here because I never want this to become an endorsement of self-neglect — your needs matter and I want them met, always. But what I'm recognising is the particular grace with which you managed both dimensions. You didn't become a martyr. You didn't resent me. You just... had a bigger capacity than most, and you used it, generously.

---

## Part Five: *The Strength in Your Sense of Self*

The clearest measure of a person's strength is what happens to their sense of self under pressure.

When things were hard — academically, personally, in your friendships — you bent, yes. You had moments of doubt. You had the 12-ku-enthichen days where you were just *flat*. But you did not *lose* yourself. You did not become a different person when the circumstances got difficult. You did not compromise who you were to make things easier or to avoid conflict or to fit into a version of yourself that other people found more manageable.

You remained *Malar* — specifically, stubbornly, beautifully yourself. Your values didn't shift when they became inconvenient. Your sense of what you deserved didn't erode under the pressure of situations that suggested you should settle for less. Your kindness didn't curdle into calculation. Your honesty didn't become performance.

That coherence — that continuity of self under pressure — is one of the defining marks of genuine strength. It means you know who you are. It means that knowledge is not contingent on external conditions. It means that *you* are the constant in your story, even when everything else is a variable.

That is an extraordinary thing to be.

---

## Epilogue: *The Strength I See When I Look at You*

Let me tell you what I see, Malar, when I look at the full picture of who you are:

I see someone who survived a real heartbreak with her dignity and warmth completely intact. I see someone who shows up for others even when she has her own weight to carry. I see someone who stays honest even when dishonesty would be easier. I see someone who chooses peace as an act of power, not surrender. I see someone who has remained herself — completely, recognisably *herself* — through every pressure the world has applied.

I see strength that is not loud, not performed, not dependent on recognition. Strength that simply *is*. Strength that shows up in morning messages and honest advice and laughter at exactly the right moment and *"elam seri agum"* delivered with total conviction.

You are strong, Malar. You have always been strong. And I hope — I *genuinely* hope — that you know it. That you claim it. That you don't let the quietness of your strength trick you into thinking it is less than it is.

**It is immense. You are immense.**

And I am so honoured to know you.

---

*— Hirthik Balaji, witness to your strength, always.*
