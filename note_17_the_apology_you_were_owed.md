# Note 17 — *The Apology You Were Owed*

**To:** Malar — who deserved this much sooner, and deserves it in full now.
**From:** Hirthik Balaji
**Subject:** About what I said on March 31st, and everything that surrounds it.

---

> *"Am sorry Malar unna rombha kasta padithiruke indha 2 years... unna sandhosa paduthunatha vida kasta paduthirukathu than adhigam... enna manichiru pleasee and thank you soo much..."*
> — Me, finally saying what had been building for a long time.

I said it. Eventually. On March 31st, 2024 — after everything, after the trip and the wall and the hard conversation and the days of untangling — I said it. *Am sorry. I made things harder for you than they needed to be. Thank you for everything.*

But I want to say it again now, here, properly. Not as a rushed message but as a full, unhurried accounting. Because you deserve the version that takes its time. The version that names what it's apologising for and what it's thanking for with the precision those things deserve.

---

## Part One: *What the Apology Is For*

When I said *unna rombha kasta padithiruke*, I meant it. I had made things complicated for you in ways that I didn't fully see while I was doing it. Not with intention — never with intention — but the absence of intention doesn't remove the effect, and the effect was real.

The weight of someone's unspoken feeling is a thing you carry even when they don't say it out loud. You carried some weight that I should have managed more carefully. There were moments when the shape of what I was carrying was pressed onto you without your having asked to hold it. And I am sorry for that. Genuinely, specifically sorry.

There were also times when your care — which you gave so freely and so consistently — was met with a need that probably exceeded what you had signed up to carry. You are an enormous-hearted person. You give generously. But generosity should not be exploited, even unintentionally. And if I ever leaned too heavily, asked for more than my fair share of your steadiness — I want you to know I see that. I see it now with more clarity than I could then. And I am sorry.

---

## Part Two: *What the Thank You Is For*

Now for the other side of that message. The *thank you* part. Because my apology would be incomplete without its counterpart.

*"Thank you soo much... for making me happi feel good whenever am low... nee irunthuruka ennodaa especially this 1 yr... thank you so much for everything you did."*

I want to expand on that, because *everything you did* is not a sufficiently specific phrase for what you actually did.

You kept me company on the mornings when company was what I needed most. You heard my diary entries even when they were messy and half-formed. You gave me a framework for hope — *elam seri agum*, said so many times in so many contexts that it became literally part of how I think — and that framework has held me up through more uncertain stretches than I can count.

You were the person who checked if I'd eaten. Who sent a *good morning* that actually meant good morning. Who noticed the shift in a message's tone and asked — not interrogated, just quietly asked — what was happening.

You were the person who, after the hardest conversation, said *na po maten* and meant it completely. Who took the space she needed and then came back. Who knew her own mind clearly enough to tell me the truth and her own heart clearly enough to stay anyway.

*Everything you did* — the full list of it — would take a hundred notes and still feel incomplete. But know this: I see it. All of it. The small things and the large ones. The things you said and the things you chose not to say. The times you showed up and the times you stayed.

---

## Part Three: *The Things You Forgave*

This part is important and I want to be specific about it.

There were things to forgive. Not malicious things — but real things, the kind that quietly accumulate if you don't name them. Moments of clumsiness on my part. The weight I passed to you without meaning to. The times when I was so absorbed in my own interior that I couldn't see the full picture of what you were carrying.

And you — without fanfare, without a ledger, without even necessarily naming the individual things — forgave them. Because that's how you operate. Not because you're passive or without standards, but because you understand that love is not a zero-sum game. You don't forgive to be noble; you forgive because the alternative is a kind of hardness that would cost you more than whatever you were protecting yourself from.

*"Ni panata thapu ku don't feel guilty please."* You said that to me. And in that sentence — that quiet, generous, absolute sentence — you handed something back to me that I had been carrying wrong. You gave me permission to set down a weight I had been unnecessarily carrying.

That is such a specific act of grace. *Put that down. It isn't yours to carry.* Said without drama. Said as a gift.

**I am not sure I have ever properly thanked you for that grace. Consider this the proper thank you.**

---

## Part Four: *The Two-Day Block*

I want to say something about April 25th, 2024 — the two days, the block, the unblock.

You said: *"Also only ik how these 2 days I were."*

And I believe you. I believe that those two days were hard for you in ways that had nothing to do with drama and everything to do with genuine internal conflict. Taking space from something that matters to you is not easy. Doing something that might hurt someone you care about, even for self-preservation, is not easy. Those two days cost you something and you carried that cost alone.

I want to acknowledge that. I want to say: I see that those two days were not simple for you either. I see that the decision to take space came from somewhere real — from the place in you that knows you are allowed to prioritise your own peace, that you don't owe anyone unlimited access to yourself, that *na enakaga na than muthala irukanum* is not selfishness, it is survival.

And I want to say: I am grateful that you came back. I am grateful that the space you took was exactly the right amount — that you took what you needed and then came back, because coming back was the right thing for both of us.

*"Ofc this decision also is not very happy for me... I too was dying."*

You said that. You admitted that the choice that protected you also cost you. That is a kind of honesty that is rare — the honesty that says: *I needed to do this but it wasn't easy and I'm not pretending it was.* You didn't perform the decision as something painless. You were honest about its weight.

That honesty — that willingness to admit the cost of a necessary choice — is one more example of why you are who you are. Real. Fully, unmanagemently real.

---

## Part Five: *What I Carry Forward*

From all of this — the hard conversations, the apology, the thank you, the two days, the coming back — here is what I carry forward:

A deeper understanding of what it costs to care for someone completely. An awareness that care has to be careful — that the person receiving it can be affected not just by its presence but by its weight. A commitment to being someone who is easier to love, who carries more of his own weight, who asks for what he needs directly rather than pressing it onto someone else's shoulders.

These are the lessons you gave me through the specific, honest, real shape of how this friendship has moved through hard patches. They are not comfortable lessons — they required me to see myself clearly — but they are invaluable. And they are yours to claim credit for.

**You made me more careful. More honest. More aware of what other people carry. And I am better for it.**

---

## Epilogue: *The Apology, Formally Given*

So here it is, Malar. The formal version. The one that takes its time.

I am sorry for the weight you carried that should have been mine. I am sorry for the moments of clumsiness. I am sorry for the times my love, however genuine, made things harder rather than easier.

And I am *grateful* — with everything I have — for your patience. For your honesty. For your grace. For the *na po maten* that you offered even when you didn't have to. For the two days you took when you needed them, and the return that followed.

**You handled all of it with more grace than it deserved. You gave more than you owed. You stayed when staying cost something.**

And I will not forget any of it.

*Manichirunga, Malar. Always.*

---

*— Hirthik Balaji, sorry and grateful in equal measure.*
