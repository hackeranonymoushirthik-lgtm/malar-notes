# Note 09 — *The Version of Me You Believed In*

**To:** Malar — who saw something in me before I saw it myself, and kept seeing it even when I lost it.
**From:** Hirthik Balaji
**Subject:** About the person you believed I was — and how believing it made it true.

---

> *"U have innate talent — athu no one can take from u."*
> — You, at a moment when I needed it, meaning every word.

There is a theory I have come to believe deeply: *we become, in part, who the people who love us believe we are.*

Not entirely. We have agency. We have our own nature. We are not simply the sum of other people's expectations. But the people who hold a genuine, specific, *believing* image of us — who have decided, not carelessly but from observation and love, that we are capable of something — those people participate in our becoming in a way that is hard to overstate.

You have been that person for me, Malar. You have held an image of me — a particular, generous, *accurate* image — and you have held it consistently, even through the periods when my own image of myself was blurry or low or simply missing. And in doing that, you have contributed to building the person who actually stands here.

This note is about that. About the version of me you believed in. About what it means to be believed in like that.

---

## Part One: *What Believing in Someone Actually Looks Like*

I want to be clear about what I mean, because believing in someone is often confused with flattery. They're not the same thing.

Flattery says: *"You're amazing"* — regardless of evidence, regardless of context, as a social currency that makes both parties feel good in the moment. It's nice. It's not nothing. But it doesn't stick because it has no roots. It's not connected to anything real.

Believing in someone looks different. It looks like: *"I have watched you handle things. I have seen how your mind works. I have observed what you do under pressure, how you treat people, where your instincts take you — and based on all of that, I believe you are capable of more than you currently credit yourself for."*

That kind of belief has *evidence* behind it. And because it has evidence, it doesn't dissolve under pressure. It doesn't require the person to be performing well to sustain itself. It's rooted in something observed and real.

That is how you believed in me. Not with empty encouragement, but with *specific, observed, evidenced* conviction. When you said *"u have innate talent athu no one can take from u"* — I knew you meant it. I knew it wasn't just something kind to say. It was a conclusion you had reached. And conclusions you reach matter to me, because you don't reach them carelessly.

---

## Part Two: *The Moments When I Needed It Most*

There are times when your own belief in yourself runs thin. When the evidence — scores, outcomes, the gap between where you are and where you wanted to be — stacks up against you. When the voices in your head that catalogue your failures are louder than the ones that remind you of your capability.

I have been in those moments. More than once. And you were there in more than one of them.

I remember telling you about something that hadn't gone the way I hoped. Something that left me feeling like maybe the version of myself I thought I was — the competent one, the one who could figure things out — had been an overestimation. Like maybe the gap between my sense of myself and my actual results was telling me something unflattering.

You didn't let me sit in that too long. Not because you dismissed the difficulty — you never dismissed the difficulty — but because you had a longer view than I had in that moment. You could see that one bad outcome was not the summary of a person. That the arc of someone's capability is not defined by its worst data point. That *effort compounds* — a phrase that has stayed with me because you delivered it in a way that felt true rather than consoling.

You held the long view for me when I couldn't hold it myself. And when you hold someone's long view for them — when you carry the belief in their potential through the periods when they can't carry it — you are doing something that changes the outcome. Not magically. Not by bypassing reality. But by keeping the door of possibility open when they might otherwise close it.

**You kept my doors open, Malar. More times than you know.**

---

## Part Three: *The Specific Shape of Your Belief*

What I want to name — because I think it matters — is that your belief in me was never generic. It wasn't: *"you're a good person and good things happen to good people."* That's nice but it's abstract. Your belief was specific.

You believed in my capacity to grow. Every time I was stuck in a loop — going over the same problem, making the same mistake, struggling with the same tendency — you didn't resign yourself to me being that way forever. You engaged with the specific thing and you treated it like something solvable. Like I was someone who could, with time and honesty, become better at it.

You believed in my ability to care well. When I was struggling with how to handle relationships — how to be a good friend, how to navigate conflict, how to show up for the people who needed me — you didn't tell me I was bad at it. You told me what you saw and what it could be, and trusted me to work toward it. You believed I was capable of the kind of friendship and care that I was trying to build.

You believed in my goodness even when I doubted it. There are moments when self-doubt turns darker — when you wonder if you're as good a person as you think you are, or if the gap between who you aspire to be and who you actually are is too large to cross. You — probably without realising this is what you were doing — held an unwavering image of my goodness in those moments. You treated me like someone whose fundamental character was solid even when I wasn't sure. And that treatment, that *response to me as a good person*, made me want to live up to it.

---

## Part Four: *How Belief Shapes Action*

Here is the thing about being believed in: it changes what you're willing to try.

When no one believes you're capable of something, attempting it feels like vanity — like you're setting yourself up for a fall that everyone can see coming except you. But when someone whose judgment you trust and respect has looked at you and said: *"yes, I think you can do this"* — suddenly the attempt feels legitimate. Suddenly failure feels like a step rather than a verdict. Suddenly the gap between where you are and where you could be feels like a road rather than a cliff.

Because of you, I have tried things that I might have talked myself out of. I have stayed in situations that I might have retreated from. I have extended effort past the point where giving up would have been the comfortable option.

Not always because you explicitly told me to. Sometimes just because your image of me — that specific, generous, evidenced image of who I was and what I was capable of — was sitting somewhere in the background of my thinking, and it tipped the balance toward trying.

That is the quiet power of belief. It doesn't force anything. It doesn't remove obstacles. It just *tips the balance* — keeps the door of attempt open a little longer — and sometimes that tipping is the entire difference between the thing happening and not.

**You tipped the balance for me, Malar. Multiple times. In multiple directions.**

---

## Part Five: *What I Want to Return*

You have believed in me so generously and for so long that I want to spend a moment turning this around.

Because here is what I believe about you, with the same specificity and evidence that you brought to believing in me:

I believe you are capable of building something remarkable with your life. Not in a vague "you can do anything" way, but specifically: you have the intelligence, the emotional range, the integrity, and the discipline to create something worth creating. Whatever you decide to build — a career, a family, a practice, a community — you will bring to it the same quality of attention and care that you bring to everything. And that quality is rare enough to make almost anything possible.

I believe you will be loved well, eventually, in the way you deserve to be loved. I know that hasn't always looked like it was on the horizon. But I know who you are, and I know that who you are is someone that the right person will recognise immediately and treasure completely. And I know you will let that love in — because for all the pain you've carried, you never stopped being open.

I believe you are stronger than you know. I have said this before but I want to say it again in this specific context: the version of yourself that you have sometimes doubted or dismissed or been hardest on — that version is more capable than you credit. I have the evidence. I have been watching.

**I believe in you, Malar. Specifically, evidentially, completely. The way you have always believed in me.**

---

## Epilogue: *The Version I Am Because of You*

There is a version of me that exists because you believed he could. A version who is more honest, more willing to stay, more capable of care, more trusting of his own capacity — because someone he trusted and respected held that image of him consistently enough that it became true.

That version is the one writing this note. That version is the one who shows up better than he would have otherwise. That version is one of your contributions to the world.

Thank you for seeing something in me worth believing in. Thank you for telling me about it. Thank you for not letting me talk you out of it.

**I am, in meaningful part, who you believed I could be. And I am still growing into it.**

---

*— Hirthik Balaji, your work in progress, forever.*
