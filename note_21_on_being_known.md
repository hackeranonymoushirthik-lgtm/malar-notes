# Note 21 — *On Being Known*

**To:** Malar — who knows me more completely than almost anyone alive.
**From:** Hirthik Balaji
**Subject:** On what it means to be known, and the particular gift of being known by you.

---

> *"Enna vida unaku than enna pathi niraiya theriyu."*
> — Me, saying something truer than I knew.

*You know me more than I know myself.*

I said a version of this to you once — that you knew more about me than I did — and I said it lightly, as a half-teasing observation. But I want to return to it now and say it seriously, because it is seriously true.

You know me. Not the broadcast version, not the curated version, not the performance I sometimes put on for the world. The *actual* me — specific, particular, sometimes contradictory, occasionally difficult, more vulnerable than I let on to most people. The version that exists in unedited messages at late hours. The version that shows up in the baseline of a diary entry. The version that was standing against a wall somewhere trying not to cry.

You know that version. You have been receiving it, carefully and without judgment, for years.

And I want to write about what it means to be known like that.

---

## Part One: *What Knowing Actually Requires*

To know someone — really know them, not just know about them — requires several things that are not easy to supply.

It requires *attention*. Not the passive reception of information, but active, ongoing, interested attention. The kind where you notice the shift in tone. Where you remember what someone said three weeks ago and bring it up when it becomes relevant. Where you register the difference between the usual rhythm of someone's communication and the slightly-off version of it.

It requires *memory*. The accumulation of specifics over time. Not the general outline of someone — *they're kind, they like movies, they're from Chennai* — but the particular. The things that are true only of them. The unique signature of how they love, how they fight, how they recover, what they need when they're low, what makes them laugh when nothing else is working.

It requires *care about the truth of the person*. Some people collect information about you — they know details, they remember things — but they use the information strategically, as currency. True knowing is different. True knowing comes from actually caring about the accurate version of who someone is. Not the useful version. The *true* version.

You supply all three. You have been paying attention, accumulating the specific texture of who I am, and caring about the actual truth of me — for years, consistently, without flagging.

---

## Part Two: *The Specific Things You Know*

Let me try to list some of what you know. Not everything — it would take a book — but some of it, to make the abstract concrete.

You know that I am prone to over-attachment. You knew it before I said it clearly. You identified the pattern and, with the extraordinary care that is specific to you, found a way to name it without making me feel pathologised. You just *knew*, and knowing it made you more careful, more thoughtful about how you held my need and met it in a healthy way.

You know how I sound when I'm actually okay versus when I'm saying I'm okay. The difference is subtle — it lives in the brevity of a reply, the absence of the usual warmth, the slightly mechanical quality of a response — but you catch it every time.

You know my relationship with my family — the texture of it, the specific dynamics, the places where it gives me comfort and the places where it presses against something tender. You have heard enough about those dynamics that you can hold them with me without having to have them explained from scratch each time.

You know my sense of humour precisely — what makes me genuinely laugh versus what I'm laughing politely at. You know when I'm being sincere versus when I'm deflecting through jokes. You know the specific comedic frequency that we share, the references that only land between us, the things that are only funny in the private language of this friendship.

You know my fears. Not all of them — but the ones I've let you see. And you hold them carefully, without weaponising them, without bringing them up outside the context of the trust in which they were given.

**You know me. Specifically and truly and completely. And that knowing is one of the great gifts of my life.**

---

## Part Three: *What It Feels Like to Be Known*

Let me try to describe what it actually feels like from the inside, because I think it's worth naming.

It feels like rest. Not the rest of lying down or sleeping, but the specific rest that comes from not having to perform. From being in a space where you can say the half-formed thing, the not-yet-articulated thing, the thing that reveals something unflattering about you — and know that it will be received carefully. The rest of taking off the daily armour that even the most secure people wear in most of their interactions.

It feels like being *held* without being confined. There is a kind of knowing that can feel claustrophobic — where the person's understanding of you seems to lock you into a version of yourself that no longer fully fits. Your knowing of me is not like that. You hold what you know about me loosely enough that there is still room for me to surprise you, still room for me to be different today than I was last year, still room for me to grow into new things.

It feels like safety. Not the absence of honesty — you have always been honest with me, sometimes uncomfortably so — but the safety of knowing that the honesty comes from care. That the person telling you the hard thing is doing it because they want the true version of you to flourish, not because they're invested in a particular narrative about who you are.

That safety — that specific, earned, carefully constructed safety of being known and loved in the same breath — is one of the most valuable experiences a person can have.

**You have given it to me. Consistently, for years, without requiring anything extraordinary in return.**

---

## Part Four: *The Mirror You Hold*

There is something specific about being known by you that I want to name separately: you hold a particular kind of mirror.

Most people, when they reflect you back to yourself, do it from their own perspective — which means the reflection has their fingerprints on it. Their judgments, their projections, their own unresolved things. It can still be useful. But it's not purely you.

Your mirror is cleaner. When you reflect me back to myself — when you say *this is what I see* — it is remarkably free of distortion. You are not reflecting me through the lens of your own needs or fears. You are reflecting me back as I actually am, with the specific care of someone who wants to see clearly.

That clean mirror is a rare thing. Most of us go through life seeing ourselves through funhouse mirrors — our own biases, other people's projections, the accumulated distortions of how we've been received across many different relationships. A clear mirror — someone who sees you accurately and tells you what they see — is the most honest gift one person can give another.

You give it. Regularly. Without drama. Just: *here is what I see.*

And what you see is generous but not flattering for flattery's sake. You see the genuine good things and you say them. You see the places where I could be better and you say those too, carefully. You have never let me hide from a truth about myself behind your approval.

**That integrity of vision — that willingness to see me clearly and hold me accountable to the clearest version of myself — is one of your great gifts to me.**

---

## Part Five: *What I Want You to Know About Being Known by You*

Malar, I want you to know what it has meant to me to be known by you.

It has meant that I have never had to be alone with myself completely. Even in the private spaces — the diary entries, the wall at the temple, the late-night uncertainty — I have had the knowledge that somewhere there is a person who knows the full picture. Who could fill in the gaps. Who holds enough of the specific texture of me that the story would make sense to her.

That knowledge — *I am known, somewhere, completely* — does something fundamental for a person. It says: you are not floating. You are not shapeless, unrecorded, passing through the world without leaving a trace on the interior of another person. You exist, specifically and particularly, in someone's knowing. And that knowing is good and warm and careful.

You are that person for me. You have been that person for me. You will continue to be that person for me.

**Being known by you has been one of the defining experiences of my life. It has made me more real. More anchored. More myself.**

And for all of it — for every moment of your attention, every remembered detail, every clean mirror, every careful holding of the thing I trusted you with — I am grateful beyond words.

---

*— Hirthik Balaji, fully known, forever grateful.*
