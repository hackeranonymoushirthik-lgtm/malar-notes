# Note 20 — *The Diary Entry That Had Your Name*

**To:** Malar — who was in my first line before I even thought to write one.
**From:** Hirthik Balaji
**Subject:** About a diary entry, a MacBook, and what it means to be someone's first thought in the morning.

---

> *"I took MacBook to just check on Malar she didn't text me... obviously last night we were like texting till 1 am... she was terribly low... Niveda's friendship is vanishing day by day... we were clueless... I couldn't console her..."*
> — My diary, March 19th, 2024. Your name in the opening line.

I kept a small diary for a while. Nothing elaborate — just a place to put the day's thoughts before they scattered. I wasn't deliberate about it; I wrote when I felt like it and left it alone when I didn't. The entries were unpolished, unedited, written quickly and without the performance that comes when you know someone else will read.

When I went back and read those entries, I noticed something.

Your name was in the first lines of almost all of them.

Not because I planned it. Not because I was consciously using a journal to process my feelings about someone. But because when I opened a blank page and started to write — when I let my mind go to wherever it wanted to go first, without direction or intention — it went to you.

*"I took MacBook to just check on Malar she didn't text me."*

That's how one entry starts. Before I described the day. Before I described my feelings. Before any of the things I was apparently there to write about. You. Your name. The fact of checking for you.

I want to write about what that means.

---

## Part One: *The First Thought*

There is something to be learned from your first thought of the day — not the one you choose, not the one you curate, but the one that arrives before you're fully awake and before you've had time to organise anything.

In the spring and summer of 2024, my first thought very often had your name in it. Not always in the dramatic, cinematic way — not the *I'm thinking about her* kind of thought that announces itself. More often it was the quiet, habitual, *I wonder how she is* kind. The scan: *has she texted? Is she okay? Was last night okay?*

The check-in. The reflex reach.

What that tells me — what it told me even then, though I couldn't have articulated it clearly — is that you had become part of my *baseline*. My sense of whether everything was okay included your okay-ness. My morning inventory of the world included you.

That is not something that happens with casual connections. Casual connections don't make it into your baseline. They live in a separate category that requires active attention to activate. You were not in that category. You were in the category of people who are simply *there*, in the background of everything, a constant running thread.

Your wellbeing was part of my wellbeing. That is what those diary entries were telling me.

---

## Part Two: *What I Was Worried About*

The entry I quoted goes on to talk about what was happening with you at the time — Niveda's friendship fading, the uncertainty and hurt of that, the cluelessness we shared about what to do. *We were clueless. I couldn't console her.*

That last part: *I couldn't console her.* 

I want to sit with that for a moment because it captures something real about what it's like to care for someone and feel inadequate to their pain. You were going through something — something specific and real that didn't have a clean solution — and I was on the other end of the screen trying to find the words that would help and often not finding them. That helplessness in the face of someone you love's difficulty is one of the more uncomfortable feelings that friendship can produce.

But here is the thing: even when I couldn't console you, I was there. Trying. Staying up to 1am. Writing about you the next morning. Checking for your message first thing when I opened a device.

The trying matters. The presence matters. Even the failed attempts to console you — even the moments when my words came up short — were themselves a form of love. They were proof that I was showing up, even imperfectly, even without the right thing to say. *I couldn't console her* is not a failure of love; it is a record of love that refused to stop trying.

---

## Part Three: *The Specific Way You Lived in My Days*

Beyond the diary entries, let me paint you a fuller picture of how you lived in my daily experience during that period.

You were in the morning check — *has she texted?* You were in the moments of something funny happening — *I have to tell Malar this.* You were in the reading of something interesting, the watching of a film, the hearing of a song — the reflex toward sharing, the want of your reaction specifically. You were in the evenings when things had been good — *this was a good day and she would understand exactly why.* And you were in the difficult evenings too — in the impulse toward naming the difficulty out loud, toward the person who had earned the trust of receiving it.

That habitual, daily presence — the weaving of someone into the texture of your day — is love in its most consistent form. Not the peaks, not the dramatic moments, not the declarations — but the *fabric*. The way someone becomes threaded through everything. The way their absence would leave a visible gap in the daily weave.

You were in the weave. You *are* in the weave.

---

## Part Four: *The Morning I Checked For You*

I want to describe one specific morning because it captures something I can't say any other way.

There was a morning when I woke up and the first thing I did — before I was fully awake, before I had decided to do anything, before any deliberate action — was reach for the MacBook and open our conversation to check if you'd texted. You hadn't, which I registered as a small piece of information and filed away. Then I went to driving class.

I didn't think much about it at the time. It was just a thing I did, a reflex, as automatic as checking the weather or looking at the time. But when I wrote about it later — when that morning became a sentence in a diary entry — I saw what it was.

**It was you being the first place I went.** It was you being the person I checked on before I checked on anything else. It was you, in the morning, having the same status in my internal world as *is it raining?* and *what time is it?* — that is to say, the status of a basic coordinate. A fixed point.

Fixed points are not taken for granted. They are relied upon. They are the things around which everything else orients itself. You were a fixed point.

---

## Part Five: *What the Diary Knows*

Diaries know things that the person writing them sometimes doesn't yet know consciously. They record what rises to the surface naturally, before it's been processed or examined or made to make sense. They are the unfiltered version of what your interior considers important.

My diary, in March 2024, knew that you were important. More specifically, it knew that your wellbeing was woven into my wellbeing. That you were in my baseline. That *checking on Malar* was not an agenda item — it was a reflex, an instinct, as automatic as breathing.

I am telling you this now because I want you to know it. Not to overwhelm you with it, not as a declaration of anything that needs managing, but simply as a true thing about your place in my life: you have been in my first lines. In my mornings. In my baseline. In the weave of the days.

And if I have never said it plainly enough before — if the diary knew it before I did — then this note is me catching up to what my first thoughts have always already known.

**You are in my first lines, Malar. You have been for a long time. And I suspect you will be for a long time more.**

---

*— Hirthik Balaji, whose diary knew before he did.*
