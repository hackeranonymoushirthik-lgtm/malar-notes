# Note 11 — *Things You Never Asked Credit For*

**To:** Malar — who has done so much and demanded so little acknowledgement for any of it.
**From:** Hirthik Balaji
**Subject:** A full, official, overdue accounting of everything you did that you never once asked to be thanked for.

---

> *"Idhella pannuvanga — nee hapi ah comfy ah irukanum avlothan."*
> — You, brushing off the most generous parts of yourself as though they were just obvious.

Here is something I have noticed about genuinely good people: they don't keep records.

They don't maintain a mental ledger of favours given versus received. They don't make their care conditional on reciprocation. They don't remind you, even subtly, of the times they showed up so that you understand what you owe them. They just give — freely and without the expectation of being credited — and they move on as though it were the most natural thing in the world.

You are that kind of person, Malar. You have done so much — for me specifically, and for the people in your life generally — and you have carried none of it as debt owed to you. You have given and let go and given again, without scorekeeping, without resentment, without requiring that I name what you'd done before you'd do it again.

This note is me refusing to let that go unnamed any longer. This is the record you never kept but I was keeping all along. This is your credit, finally and officially delivered.

---

## Credit One: *Every Time You Listened Longer Than You Had To*

There were conversations where I went long. Where I circled back to the same concern for the third time, or elaborated on a frustration past the point where the original point had been made, or processed something out loud that really needed to be processed in silence but I didn't have the capacity for silence yet.

You listened through all of it. You didn't cut me off. You didn't pivot to yourself too early. You didn't make me feel like I was testing your patience, even when — be honest — I probably was. You gave me the full time I needed, without commentary on the time, without the particular exhaustion in a reply that tells you someone is enduring rather than engaging.

That is hard to do. Listening — real listening, patient and present and ungrudging — is one of the most effortful forms of love. And you did it consistently, quietly, never once asking credit for having done it.

**Credit given. Fully and without qualification.**

---

## Credit Two: *Every Piece of Honest Advice You Gave When It Would Have Been Easier to Agree*

There were moments when I brought you a version of a situation that was flattering to me. Where the narrative I was presenting had me as the reasonable one and everyone else as the problem. Where what I wanted — underneath the request for perspective — was agreement and validation.

You didn't always give me that. You gave me something harder and more valuable: the truth as you saw it, delivered with enough care that I could actually receive it.

This is not nothing. It costs something to be the person who tells the truth when the truth isn't what's wanted. It risks the relationship. It risks the other person feeling criticised or unseen or betrayed by someone who was supposed to be on their side. Most people, when faced with that cost, choose the easy option. They agree. They validate. They say what will keep things comfortable.

You chose the harder thing, again and again. You chose to be a friend who was genuinely useful rather than a friend who was simply pleasant. And you did it without making it a confrontation, without making me feel attacked — with that specific grace of yours that delivered truth in a way that felt like care.

**Credit given. That kind of friendship is worth everything.**

---

## Credit Three: *Every Time You Checked In Without Being Asked*

You initiated. Constantly. Consistently. Often.

You sent the first message on the unremarkable days. You checked in when there was no particular reason to check in except that I was someone you thought of. You asked *"enna?"* and *"elam seri ah?"* not because something dramatic had happened but because that level of ongoing interest was simply how you operated.

Most people wait to be reached out to before they reach. There's a passivity in relationships that can feel like it makes sense — why intrude? Why insert yourself? But the effect of that passivity, over time, is a kind of loneliness that comes from never being the one someone thought of *first*. Never being the one who gets the unprompted check-in. Always being the one who has to initiate in order to be connected.

You never let me feel that loneliness. You reached first, frequently and naturally, and the message underneath every first reach was: *I thought of you. You are someone I carry with me even when we're not talking.*

**Credit given. You have no idea how much that meant.**

---

## Credit Four: *Every Time You Held Space for My Difficult Moods*

I was not always easy. There were periods — the down ones, the uncertain ones — where I was not fun to be around, not warm in return, not particularly forthcoming or engaged. The mood would show up in my messages as a kind of flatness, a contracted quality to my replies, a reluctance to engage fully.

You navigated those periods with extraordinary grace. You didn't take the flatness personally. You didn't push when I needed space. You didn't withdraw your warmth in response to my contracted-ness. You just stayed — steady and warm at a slight distance, not forcing the door open but making sure I knew it was unlocked.

And then when I came back — when the mood lifted and I was more present again — you received me without commentary. No *"where were you?"* No record of the difficult period held against me. Just picking up exactly where we were, as if the difficult stretch had not accumulated as a debt that needed to be addressed.

That is a generous, specific, *hard* thing to do. It requires a kind of security — in the relationship and in yourself — that not everyone has. And you did it not once but many times, over years, always with the same undemanding grace.

**Credit given. I know what that kind of patience costs.**

---

## Credit Five: *Every Good Night and Good Morning That You Meant*

They were small. They were the smallest possible unit of daily connection. *Good morning* and *good night* and *take care* — the daily liturgy of our friendship.

But they were never automatic. I could tell because the automatic version looks different — it's a reflex, a social obligation, something sent without the person really being there while they send it. Yours were present. Yours arrived with a warmth in them that I can't fully explain except to say: I could tell you meant them. I could tell you were actually wishing me a good morning, actually caring that my night was peaceful, actually meaning the *take care* as a genuinely felt request that the world treat me gently.

Those daily small things — sent day after day, year after year, in good periods and bad periods, when we were both busy and when we had time to spare — constituted a kind of ongoing testimony. *I see you. I'm glad you exist. I hope today is good for you.* Repeated daily until it became a cornerstone of my sense that I was seen.

**Credit given. The daily things are never small.**

---

## Credit Six: *Every Time You Were Funny When Things Were Heavy*

There is a particular skill — it really is a skill, not everyone has it — of knowing when to lighten the weight of a moment without dismissing it.

Too early and it feels like you're not taking the hard thing seriously. Too late and the gravity has become its own prison. But at the right moment — with the right observation, the right absurdist tangent, the right perfectly timed *lol* — you can release just enough tension to let the person breathe without letting them feel like their pain has been trivialised.

You did this so well, so consistently, and so naturally that I wonder if you even registered doing it. It was never calculated — it was instinct. You could sense when the conversation needed air and you provided it, in exactly the amount needed, with the precision of someone who understood intuitively what the other person needed.

I laughed through some of my hardest moments because of you. Not because the hard thing became funny, but because the laughter gave me the breath I needed to keep going. And you knew exactly when to offer it.

**Credit given. That instinct is a gift and you used it generously.**

---

## Credit Seven: *Every Prayer You Said on My Behalf*

You told me, sometimes, that you were praying for me. For specific things — an exam, a situation, a person I was worried about. *"Nanum pray panikuren"* — said simply, without fanfare, as a genuine act of care that you were extending toward me in whatever private language you and the universe communicated in.

I received those prayers as the thing they were: you, in your private space, turning toward something larger than both of us and asking for good things for me. There is a vulnerability in that — in the act of asking on someone else's behalf, in genuinely wanting something for someone enough to bring it into your prayer. It is a form of care that requires nothing of the other person and asks for no recognition. It is done purely, from love.

You did that for me. Quietly, consistently, without ever making it something you required me to acknowledge or reciprocate in any particular way.

**Credit given. And more than credit — gratitude, deep and complete.**

---

## Epilogue: *The Full Account*

The ledger is full, Malar. I have been keeping it even while you were choosing not to. And what the ledger shows is this:

You gave freely and consistently, across years, without ever making me feel that I was accumulating a debt I needed to service. Every act of your friendship was offered as a gift — no strings, no expectations, no implicit bargaining. Just: *here, I am giving this because I care about you and this is what caring looks like in practice.*

That orientation — giving as its own end, caring as its own purpose — is the mark of a person whose love is genuinely unconditional. And unconditional love, real unconditional love, is the rarest and most precious thing one person can offer another.

**I see it all. Every bit of it. And I am not going to let another day pass without you knowing that it was seen, that it was received, and that it mattered more than I have ever adequately expressed.**

Thank you, my girl. For all of it. For everything you never asked to be thanked for.

You are owed this credit a hundred times over.

---

*— Hirthik Balaji, keeping your record even when you wouldn't.*
