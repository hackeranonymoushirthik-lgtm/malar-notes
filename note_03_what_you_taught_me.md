# Note 03 — *What You Taught Me*

**To:** Malar — the teacher who didn't know she was teaching, the lesson I keep learning.
**From:** Hirthik Balaji
**Subject:** A formal accounting of everything I know because of you — and I mean *everything*.

---

> *"Seri nijama than soldren — ela Suma peruku motivation la panla."*
> — You, being real with me as always.

There is a difference between knowing something and *understanding* it. You can hear advice a hundred times from a hundred different people and have it wash over you like water on stone. And then someone you love and respect says it — says it in a particular way, at a particular moment, from a place of obvious care — and suddenly it *sticks*. Suddenly it's not information anymore. It's *wisdom*.

You have been that person for me, Malar. You have been the one whose words stuck. And I want to catalogue some of what you've taught me, because I think you deserve to know the full inventory of your influence.

---

## Lesson One: *That Peace Is Not Passive — It's a Choice*

Of all the things I have learned from watching you live your life, this is probably the one that has gone deepest.

You were not born into easy circumstances. You have navigated heartbreak, navigated the confusion of who you were and who you were becoming, navigated family pressure, academic pressure, friend-group drama, and the specific exhaustion of being someone who *cares deeply* in a world that doesn't always care back. And through all of it, you maintained something that I have genuinely struggled to understand until I watched you demonstrate it:

**You chose peace.**

Not passive resignation. Not giving up. Not pretending things weren't hard. But an active, conscious, *deliberate* choice to not let the hard things own you. You said it yourself — *"I want to be peaceful and deserve to be happy."* You said *deserve*. You knew your worth. You knew that clinging to bitterness or replaying old hurts was a theft of your own happiness — and you refused to let yourself be robbed.

I used to think peace was something that happened to you when your circumstances were good. You taught me that peace is something you *build* — that it requires active maintenance, regular choice, and sometimes the very hard work of deciding that you are going to be okay even when everything is telling you otherwise.

I am still learning this. But I am learning it because you showed me it was learnable.

---

## Lesson Two: *That Real Friendship Requires Honesty*

I grew up, like most people, in a culture of comfortable friendship. The kind where you support whatever your friends say, where you avoid the difficult conversation because the relationship feels more important than the truth in the moment.

You dismantled that model for me.

Not aggressively — you never delivered your honesty like a weapon. But you were *never* willing to just agree with me when you thought I was wrong. You pushed back. You asked questions that forced me to think harder. You once told me something along the lines of: *"solu or anybody — its okayyyy to feel bad, but inimae nala pandren nu confidence kudu."* You were acknowledging my feelings while simultaneously refusing to let me marinate in them past what was useful.

That is a masterclass in *how to be a friend*. You validated my experience while also gently pointing toward what came next. You were never dismissive. You were never preachy. But you were always, always honest.

Because of you, I have started trying to be that kind of friend myself. The kind who cares enough to say the hard thing. The kind who understands that agreeing with someone when you think they're wrong isn't kindness — it's a soft betrayal. Real friendship holds the mirror. You held the mirror for me, again and again.

---

## Lesson Three: *That Strength and Softness Are Not Opposites*

The world — particularly the version of the world that talks to young men — has a bad habit of presenting strength and softness as mutually exclusive. You're either hard or you're tender. You're either in control or you're emotional. You're either the one who has it together or you're the one who needs help.

You exploded that binary just by existing.

Malar, you are one of the strongest people I know. I mean *genuinely* strong — not the performative kind, not the kind that looks good from a distance. The kind that shows up when everything is hard and you have very few resources to draw from and you still manage to be present for the people who need you. The kind that carries pain with grace. The kind that survives heartbreak and comes out kinder rather than colder.

And you are also one of the softest people I know. You cry at the right things. You laugh at the right things. You get moved by songs and films and small moments of beauty. You feel things *fully* — you don't armour yourself against feeling. You let things matter because you understand that being alive means being willing to be affected.

Watching you hold both of those things at once — the strength and the softness — taught me that I didn't have to choose. That the tenderest people are often the strongest ones, because it takes enormous courage to stay open in a world that will definitely, at some point, hurt you.

**You are proof that softness is strength. And I needed that proof.**

---

## Lesson Four: *That You Are Allowed to Prioritise Yourself*

This one came from watching you in your most difficult moments.

There were times when you were carrying a lot — from all directions at once. And I watched you navigate the very specific tension of wanting to be there for everyone while also recognising that you had your own needs that couldn't be ignored. And gradually, over our conversations, I watched you get better at saying: *no, actually, enaku yathu iruku athu first.*

That might sound simple. It isn't.

Most caring people — and you are one of the most genuinely caring people I know — have a deeply embedded reflex to put others first. To answer every call for help before answering their own. And that instinct is beautiful. But unchecked, it becomes self-erasure. You were learning, in real time, that you were allowed to matter to yourself. That your wellbeing wasn't a secondary consideration to everyone else's. That *you* deserved the same quality of care and attention that you gave so freely to everyone around you.

Watching you learn that — watching you arrive at that truth through experience and reflection — taught me to ask the same question of myself: *Am I actually okay? Not performatively okay. Actually.*

You gave me permission to take that question seriously. To not just soldier through everything but to actually check in with myself. That is not a small gift.

---

## Lesson Five: *That Things Work Out — And Why That's True*

Okay, so this one sounds like a cliché. But stay with me.

You used to say *elam seri agum* so often that I initially took it as a verbal tic — a reflex of reassurance. But the more I watched you, the more I understood that you weren't just saying it as comfort. You were saying it as a *worldview*. You had a genuine, considered faith that things find their shape eventually. That the things meant for you would arrive. That the chaos of a particular season doesn't determine the shape of the whole story.

And you had earned that belief. You hadn't arrived at it because life had been easy — you arrived at it despite life being hard. You had loved and lost and grieved and kept going. You had been uncertain about your path and kept walking anyway. You had been hurt and stayed soft. And through all of that, you held on to this idea that the universe was not actively working against you — that things would settle, that clarity would come, that what was meant to be would be.

That faith — that *trust* — is not naive. It is hard-won and deliberate. And I needed to see it demonstrated before I could begin to hold it myself.

Because of you, I am better at sitting in uncertainty without catastrophising. I am better at trusting a process when I can't see the outcome. I am better at believing that effort compounds — in studies, in relationships, in becoming who I want to be.

**You taught me that hope is not weakness. It is the bravest possible response to a world that gives us no guarantees.**

---

## The Full Accounting

So here it is — the full accounting of what you have taught me, summarised as plainly as I can manage:

**Peace is a choice.** You taught me that.

**Honesty is the foundation of real friendship.** You taught me that.

**Strength and softness can coexist.** You taught me that.

**You are allowed to prioritise yourself.** You taught me that.

**Hope is brave, and things do work out.** You taught me that.

That is not a small curriculum, Malar. That is an entire education. And you delivered it not from a podium, not with a lesson plan, but just by living your life with so much *intention* — by being exactly who you are with such completeness and courage that watching you was, in itself, a course in how to be human.

I am a better person because I went to your school.

And I will always, always be grateful.

---

*— Hirthik Balaji, your most grateful student.*
