# Note 18 — *100 Varusham*

**To:** Malar — my co-passenger for all one hundred.
**From:** Hirthik Balaji
**Subject:** On the night you said we'd live to a hundred together — and why I'm holding you to it.

---

> *"Rendu perum 100 vayasu va vazhvom don't worry athum onnaaa... to getherr.... seri life longg till the enddd.. pothumaaaa"*
> — Me, meaning every word, holding it like a vow.

You asked me once, in the middle of some conversation about something silly and morbid, whether I wanted to outlive you or have you outlive me. It was the kind of question that arrives in a friendly chat without warning and somehow contains within it everything important about how two people feel about each other.

My answer, if you remember, was this: *rendu perum 100 vayasu va vazhvom* — both of us, together, until we're a hundred.

It was said with a laugh. It was said quickly. But I meant it in the way that the things said quickly are sometimes the most meant — because they bypass the filter of careful presentation and arrive directly from the thing underneath.

I mean it now, with all the time in the world to mean it carefully: **100 years, Malar. Both of us. Together.**

This note is about what that means.

---

## Part One: *What It Means to Want Someone at the End*

There is a particular thing about wanting someone in the distant future — at 100, at the end, in the oldest version of your life — that says something specific about the depth of the present.

Most relationships live in the present tense. *I need you now. You are good for me in this season.* And those relationships are real and valuable and they should not be underestimated. But there is another category — the one that feels the full length of a future when it contemplates a person — the category where you can genuinely picture the person beside you not just next year but in fifty years, sixty years, all the way to the last chapter.

You are in that category for me, Malar.

When I said *100 vayasu va vazhvom*, I wasn't being hyperbolic. I was saying: the full arc of my life is better with you in it. Not just the exciting parts or the easy parts or the parts where we happen to be in the same city — but the whole arc. The long, quiet, ordinary middle. The end. All of it.

That is not a small thing to mean about someone.

---

## Part Two: *The Long History We Are Already Building*

Think about how much has already happened between us. And we are not yet even at the beginning of the long middle.

We have had the nervous, careful early conversations. We have had the first real story — yours, about Arvind, shared with a trust that I still hold carefully. We have had the boards, the exit from school, the uncertainty of what came next. We have had the complicated summer of 2024, the hard conversations, the break, the return. We have had the night before college — the long, beautiful send-off you gave me with messages that still live in my chest. We have had the shift to college, the new geography, the ways life has started pulling us into its larger orbit.

And through all of that — all those phases, all those shifts — we have remained. Not unchanged, not without friction or difficulty — but *remained*. Still here. Still texting. Still checking in. Still the person the other one calls when something needs to be held.

We are building a long history. We are already in the process of accumulating the kind of shared past that, in forty years, will look like a *life* — a whole, rich, textured relationship that spans the distance from *Hello sir* to everything that hasn't happened yet.

*100 vayasu va vazhvom.* We are already well into the construction of it.

---

## Part Three: *What I Want for You in Those 100 Years*

Since we're talking about the long arc, let me tell you what I want for you in it.

I want you to build the thing you're building in architecture school and have it stand. Literally and metaphorically. I want you to make things that outlast you — that carry your eye and your care into spaces other people inhabit. I want the buildings to have a little of Malar in them: thoughtful, specific, warm in a way that isn't always visible but always felt.

I want you to find love that is commensurate with you. The specific, real, patient, honest love that you deserve — the kind where someone sees you fully and chooses you completely and doesn't make you wonder about your worth. You have given that quality of love to so many people. You are long overdue to receive it back.

I want you to keep the specific joy you have. The joy that arrives in all caps — *SEMAAAAA* and *HAPPIHAPPI* and the triple exclamation mark — the joy that is never performative, never inflated, always exactly real. I want that joy to stay in you for all 100 years, because the world is better when you are happy in it.

I want you to keep making solo movie dates and having opinions and roasting bad films and defending the ones you love fiercely. I want you to keep the part of you that says exactly what you think, that doesn't curate herself for other people's comfort, that arrives in a conversation as fully herself.

I want you to be at peace. The specific, chosen, hard-won peace that you have been building toward — the peace of knowing who you are and what you deserve and refusing to settle for less. I want that peace to deepen with every year. I want you to be one of those people who gets *calmer* with age, not because life has ground them down but because they have grown into themselves.

---

## Part Four: *What You'll Have From Me in Those 100 Years*

Here is my end of the *100 vayasu* bargain.

I will be the person who still remembers. Who remembers that you said *Hello sir* first. Who remembers the specific shape of your voice in early morning texts. Who remembers the night before college and the things you said about my new chapter. Who remembers the honest conversation and the grace with which you held it.

I will be the person who still checks in. Less frequently than before, perhaps — life has its seasons of activity and quiet — but with the same quality of attention. The check-in that actually wants to know how you are. The *enna achu?* that isn't rhetorical. The presence that is present.

I will be the person who still talks about you the way I talk about you now — as someone indispensable, irreplaceable, at the foundational level of my story. Who you are to me will not be diminished by time or by geography or by the way lives develop in different directions. You will be, at 60 and at 80 and at 100, the person who made me believe that *elam seri agum* was a real and liveable truth.

And I will be the person who, at some unspecified point in the far future, in whatever form the world takes then, still sends a morning message. Still wants to know you're okay. Still considers you, without needing to think about it, among the most important people in his life.

**That is what I am offering in return for the hundred years. And I mean it completely.**

---

## Epilogue: *A Hundred Years is a Long Time — And Not Enough*

Here is the truth about *100 vayasu va vazhvom*: even a hundred years feels like not quite enough time with the right person. The right people — the ones who are genuinely woven into the fabric of who you are — are the ones you could keep talking to indefinitely. There is no *enough* with them. There is only more.

You are one of those people, Malar.

You are the kind of person who makes a hundred years feel like the opening act. Who makes me think: *however long the story is, I want her in it.* Not as a feature, not as a character who arrives and departs — but as a *presence*. A constant. The person who is just *there*, in all the chapters, making them better by being in them.

So here it is, official and unchangeable:

**100 vayasu va vazhvom, rendu perum.** Both of us. Together. All the way to the end. Don't try to renegotiate this because I'm not accepting revisions.

You already agreed. I heard you.

---

*— Hirthik Balaji, your co-passenger for all hundred. No returns accepted.*
