# Note 06 — *Small Things That Are Everything*

**To:** Malar — in celebration of every small thing you do that I should have named out loud a long time ago.
**From:** Hirthik Balaji
**Subject:** A love letter to the details — the specific, particular, irreplaceable things you do that make everything better.

---

> *"Good morningg ☀️"*
> — You, on mornings when I needed it most, without knowing you were delivering something.

There is a certain kind of person who believes that love is about grand gestures. The elaborate plan. The perfect speech. The cinematic moment. And those things have their place — I'm not going to pretend I haven't sent you enthusiastic texts about scenes exactly like that in films we've both watched.

But the truth — the real, lived, actual truth — is that love is mostly made of small things. Repeated small things. Consistent small things. The things that, individually, seem insignificant but collectively constitute *an entire world of care*.

This note is about those small things. Your small things. The ones I have noticed and treasured and probably never named until now.

---

## The Good Morning Messages

Let's start here because it's the most simple and probably the one I took most for granted.

You would say good morning. Consistently. Cheerfully. Sometimes with context — *"padichitu iruken"* or *"9 ku enthichen"* or just the declaration of the day beginning. And it always landed as something more than a greeting. It landed as: *"I woke up and you were someone I wanted to say hello to."*

That is not nothing. In the calculus of human connection, being someone another person thinks of when they wake up — before the day has handed them anything good or bad, before they have any reason other than genuine feeling — is something. It is a specific, beautiful something.

Every morning message from you was a small reminder that I was thought of. And on the days when I wasn't sure of much else, that reminder carried.

---

## The Way You Checked If I'd Eaten

You would ask. Regularly. *"Saptiya?"* — in the way that was somehow both casual and completely pointed. Like you were noting it down. Like my nutritional status was data you were collecting for a report.

I used to deflect from this. Sometimes I'd laugh it off. Sometimes I'd give an evasive answer. But here's what I never told you: it mattered. Every single time.

There is something about someone checking if you've eaten that goes well beyond food. It's asking: *"Are you taking care of yourself? Are you in good enough shape to make it through this day? Are you okay at the most basic physical level?"* It is the most uncomplicated form of care — and it is, in its uncomplicated way, an act of love.

You cared about whether I was fed. You cared about whether I was rested. You cared about the physical reality of my existence, not just the emotional narrative. That groundedness — that practical, everyday care — is something I hold so warmly.

---

## The Movies and the Music

Oh, the movies and the music.

Some of our best conversations were about films and songs. Not in a detached, critical-analysis way — in the fully invested, passionately opinionated, *"how dare you not feel what I feel about this"* way. You would defend a song's honour like you had a personal stake in it. You would roast a bad film with a surgical precision that genuinely impressed me. You would recommend something and then wait, barely patiently, to hear what I thought.

Those conversations — the ones where we were just two people absolutely steeped in the culture we grew up in, arguing about Vijay's filmography or getting excited about a release date or quoting something at each other — were some of the most genuinely *fun* moments I've had with anyone. And fun is underrated. Fun is its own kind of love.

You brought joy into my life through shared joy. You found the things we both loved and you lit up about them and it was impossible not to light up with you.

---

## The Way You Let Me Into Your Life

The small, everyday details of your life that you shared with me — tuition after school, the shared auto back, the fact that your amma worked mornings, the meals you were delaying or rushing, the small frustrations with friends, the small victories in biology or chemistry — those things built a world.

I knew your world. Specifically, particularly, *yours*. Not a curated highlight version. The real-time version: the tired days and the excited days and the days where nothing happened and the days where too much did.

Being let into someone's daily life — the unedited version — is one of the most intimate things one person can offer another. You gave me that. The texture of your days. The specific realness of your life. And receiving it made me feel trusted and close and genuinely connected in a way that grand declarations could never have replicated.

---

## The Times You Noticed I Was Off

You had — and have — the most precise radar.

I would send a message and somehow, in the frequency of the reply or the brevity of what I said, you would catch something. *"Enna?"* you'd ask. Or just a quiet: *"Seri ah iruka?"* And it wouldn't be intrusive — it was never intrusive — but it was always accurate. You had already assessed the situation before you asked.

Being noticed like that — being seen by someone who is actually paying attention — is one of the most profoundly comforting experiences a person can have. We live in a world of surfaces, of people who are simultaneously present and absent, whose attention is always half somewhere else. And then there is you: *fully* here, *actually* looking, noticing what the rest of the world would miss.

That attention — that particular, unhurried, loving attention — is one of the most precious gifts you have ever given me.

---

## The "Always" — and Meaning It

You said *always* in a way that meant something.

*"Always here."* *"Always praying for you."* *"Always will be there."* In someone else's mouth those could be hollow — the filler words of someone who wants to say the right thing without the commitment. In your mouth they carried weight.

Because you had demonstrated the *always* before you said it. The evidence was in every check-in, every morning greeting, every honest uncomfortable truth, every moment you stayed when you could have left, every time you showed up even when you were going through something yourself.

Your *always* was not a promise. It was a *description*. A report of what was already true. And that difference — between promising a thing and having already been doing the thing — is everything.

---

## The Laughter

I cannot write this note without giving proper attention to how much you have made me laugh.

You are *so funny*, Malar. And I don't mean politely funny — the kind where you laugh because it would be unkind not to. I mean genuinely, unexpectedly, *"I did not see that coming"* funny. Your comic timing is absurdly good. Your references are perfect. Your ability to reduce a complicated emotional situation to a single devastating observation — *lol* — is some kind of gift.

We have laughed together at things that probably don't translate to anyone else. Inside the private language of our friendship, there are a hundred tiny jokes, running references, specific ridiculous things that became shorthand. And every single one of those is a small monument to the fun of being in your company.

Laughter is bonding. Laughter is love. Laughter is the thing that makes hard stretches survivable and good stretches unforgettable.

You gave me so much of it. You *keep* giving me so much of it. And I do not take even one moment of it for granted.

---

## The Note-Passing Energy

You once suggested — with such characteristic Malar practicality — that we should write each other notes instead of texting. Pass a physical note, read it later, write one back. The idea of making our connection into something tangible, something you could hold in your hands.

I don't think we ever fully executed on that. But the *impulse* behind it has always stayed with me. The impulse to make care into something *real*. To give it physical form. To say: *here, this is not just data moving through a phone — this is me, thinking of you, making something for you.*

That impulse is so you. The desire to make love *tactile*, to make it *real*, to give it substance beyond the digital and transient. And in that spirit — in the spirit of making the feeling concrete — these notes exist.

These notes are the thing I never handed you when you suggested we should do it. Late, yes. But finally here.

---

## Epilogue: *The Small Things Are the Thing*

Malar, the small things *are* the thing. Not the grand gestures. Not the significant moments. The morning messages and the food check-ins and the movie debates and the laughter and the noticing and the *always* — those are the actual substance of love in its daily form.

And you have given me so much of that daily substance. Consistently, quietly, freely, as a fundamental expression of who you are.

Every small thing you did was a choice. Every check-in, every honest word, every laugh you shared, every *"saptiya?"* — those were all moments where you could have been anywhere else, paying attention to anything else, and instead you chose *me*. Chose to show up. Chose to be present.

I see every one of those choices. I remember them. I am storing them in the place in me where the most important things live.

**Thank you for the small things, my girl. They are, and have always been, everything.**

---

*— Hirthik Balaji, paying attention back, always.*
