# Note 14 — *The Honesty That Healed*

**To:** Malar — who told me the truth when the truth was the kindest possible thing, even when it didn't feel like it.
**From:** Hirthik Balaji
**Subject:** March 21st, 2024. The day you were honest with me. And why I will be grateful for that honesty for the rest of my life.

---

> *"Na antha fake ha iruka virumbala... I'm sure ni enaku kuduthathu Vida unaku kandipa u will get 10 times higher than what u have given — I'm damn sure that's gonna happen."*
> — You, being honest AND generous at the same time, which is something only the rarest people can manage.

There is a particular kind of conversation that changes the shape of a relationship permanently — not because it broke something, but because it *revealed* something. It cleared the air of everything unspoken and let both people breathe again in a space that was suddenly, uncomfortably, beautifully honest.

We had that conversation. And I want to write about it now because I think you deserve to hear — really hear — what that conversation meant to me. Not what it felt like in the moment, which was hard. But what it *was*, in its truest form. What it showed me about who you are.

---

## Part One: *What You Could Have Done*

Let me start here, because context matters.

When I told you how I felt, you had options. Several of them. And all of them — except the one you chose — would have been easier, or at least more immediately comfortable.

You could have gone cold. You could have retreated into distance and let the silence communicate what you weren't willing to say in words. Some people do this — not from cruelty but from discomfort, from not knowing how to hold someone's honesty without either reciprocating it or running from it.

You could have been vague. You could have given me the answer that didn't fully close any doors — the "let's see," the "I don't know," the carefully uncommitted non-response that leaves someone hanging indefinitely in a limbo that is its own particular cruelty.

You could have been cold and factual and purely transactional about it. *No. Moving on.* Clean, clear, and unkind in the way that clean and clear sometimes is.

**You chose none of those things.**

What you chose was: *complete, careful, generous honesty*. You told me the truth — clearly, without false hope — and then you surrounded that truth with everything else that was also true. You told me I mattered. You told me that what I had given you was rare and real. You told me, with a specificity and a warmth that I am still sitting with: *"ni evlo efforts love care potu kuda... ik and... u mean a lot to me."*

You managed to say no while simultaneously making me feel genuinely *seen*. That is extraordinarily difficult. Most people can do one or the other. You did both.

---

## Part Two: *The Specific Generosity of What You Said*

I want to walk through some of what you said that day, because I think you may not fully understand the weight of your own words.

You said: *"I'm sure ni enaku kuduthathu Vida unaku kandipa u will get 10 times higher than what u have given — I'm damn sure that's gonna happen."*

You were in a position where you could have simply answered the question asked and been done with it. Instead, you looked ahead — to my future, to my life beyond this moment — and you said: *what you have to give is worth more than where it has gone, and someone, somewhere, will receive it in full.* You blessed my future even as you were closing a particular door in my present. What kind of person does that? What kind of person — in the middle of a conversation that was about *them*, about their feelings, about what they needed to say clearly — turns the lens to the other person's future happiness and says: *I see that for you. I believe in that for you.*

You do. That is the kind of person you are.

You also said: *"nanum athu elam paka than poren un kuda irunthae."* I want to be with you when that happens. Those words. *I want to be there.* You didn't use the honesty of this conversation as a reason to distance yourself. You said: I am *staying*. I want to *see* your happiness. I want to be there when it finds you.

That is love, Malar. Possibly purer love than the romantic kind — the love that wants your wellbeing so genuinely that it can say no to something while simultaneously staying beside you and rooting for everything good that's coming.

---

## Part Three: *What It Took*

I have thought about what it required of you to have that conversation the way you had it.

It required courage. Not the dramatic, movie-scene kind, but the quieter, more demanding kind — the courage to say something uncomfortable clearly, to not retreat into vagueness, to respect both yourself and me enough to be direct.

It required clarity. You had to know yourself — what you felt, what you didn't feel, where the line was — with enough certainty to speak it out loud. And that kind of self-knowledge doesn't come automatically. It comes from the work of actually paying attention to your own interior, which is something not everyone does and something you've always been exceptionally good at.

It required kindness. Not kindness as softness, not kindness that blurs the truth to spare feelings in the moment — but kindness as genuine care for the person in front of you, as the decision to deliver a hard thing with as much love as you can carry into it.

And it required *trust*. You had to trust me — trust that I could receive what you were saying, trust that our bond was strong enough to survive the honesty, trust that I was someone who would rather know the truth than be managed through a comfortable lie.

**You trusted me with the truth. That trust was, itself, a form of love.**

---

## Part Four: *What Happened After*

Here is the part I most want you to understand: the conversation didn't end us. It *solidified* us.

Because after that conversation, what remained was entirely real. There was nothing left to wonder about, nothing unspoken, no shape-shifting possibility hanging between us that might one day be revealed to be something other than what it appeared. What remained was the friendship — specific, honest, chosen, and clear — and that friendship stood on ground that nothing could destabilise.

You gave me something rare that day. You gave me a relationship I could fully *trust*. Not because everything was resolved in a way that felt good — it wasn't, not entirely, not immediately. But because after that conversation, I knew where I stood. I knew who we were. I knew that when you said *you mean a lot to me*, you meant it as a statement of fact and not as consolation. I knew that when you said *I'm not going anywhere*, that was the truth and not just the kind thing to say.

Clarity is a gift. You gave it to me. And in the longer arc of things, that clarity was one of the most loving things you could have done.

---

## Part Five: *The Apology I Owe You*

You said, at some point: *"am sorry na rude ha pesi iruntha nijama una hurt pananum nu ela."* And I remember reading that and thinking: *you are apologising to me?*

Malar — you have nothing to apologise for. Not a syllable. You handled one of the most delicate conversations two people can have with more grace and care and specific love than I had any right to hope for. You were honest and you were kind and you were *present* — completely and genuinely present in a conversation that could have been easier to avoid or deflect.

If anything, I owe you the apology. For the weeks of complexity before that. For whatever weight the unspoken added to your days. For any moments where the shape of my feelings made things harder for you than they should have been.

You handled all of that with more generosity than it deserved, and then you sat across from me in that conversation and you told me the truth with love, and then you *stayed*.

**I am not sorry that it happened. I am so grateful it happened the way it did. I am grateful that you were the person on the other side of it.**

---

## Epilogue: *What That Day Left Me With*

I came away from that conversation with something I didn't expect: a friendship that felt more solid than it had before. Because now it was made of only what was real. Every care, every check-in, every morning message, every *elam seri agum* — after that day, I knew with certainty that all of it was exactly what it was. No ambiguity. Just two people who had chosen each other, clearly and honestly, as the specific kind of presence in each other's lives that they actually were.

And that friendship — the real, clear, honest, post-March-21st version of what we were — has been one of the most sustaining things in my life.

It exists because you had the courage to tell me the truth. Because you were honest when you didn't have to be and generous when it would have been easier not to be and *present* when the moment required it.

**Thank you, Malar. For March 21st. For what you said and how you said it and what you chose to be after.**

You handled it like the exceptional person you are.

---

*— Hirthik Balaji, grateful for your honesty, always.*
