# Note 22 — *The Things You Said When I Was Low*

**To:** Malar — who always had the right thing, even when the right thing was hard to find.
**From:** Hirthik Balaji
**Subject:** A collection of the specific things you said to me in my lowest moments — and what each of them gave me.

---

> *"Nee oru gem maa — unnakku onum aagathuu."*
> — You, on a day when I needed to hear exactly that.

I want to write about the words you gave me when I was low.

Not the general feeling of your support — I have written about that elsewhere, and the general feeling is real and important — but the *specific* words. The particular sentences, the exact phrases, the things you said in the particular moments when they were most needed. Because I think those sentences deserve to be named and honoured individually, the way individual acts of kindness deserve acknowledgement even when they exist within a larger pattern of care.

You have said a lot of things to me over the years. Most of them I have forgotten in the way you forget the individual drops of rain, even though the rain itself shaped the landscape. But some of them I remember with the clarity of something that arrived at exactly the right moment, changed the angle of the light, and became part of how I think.

These are some of those.

---

## *"Nee oru gem maa — unnakku onum aagathuu."*

This arrived on a day when I needed it.

I had been going through something difficult — the specific details matter less than the general state of it — and I was in that place where the pain had started to talk me into conclusions that were not true. The conclusions pain draws: *something is permanently wrong. You are the problem. Things will not get better.* The low logic that arrives when you are tired and hurt and can't quite see the shape of a way through.

And you said: *you are a gem. Nothing will happen to you.*

Not *it will be fine* (though you said that too, often). Not *I understand* (though you understood completely). But a statement about my fundamental worth. *Gem* — not a performance, not an inflated compliment, but a clear, direct assertion of value. *Nothing will happen to you* — not a prediction about circumstances, but a declaration about the durability of who you were to me and who you are in the world.

You gave me my worth back in a sentence. You handed it back to me clearly when I had let pain convince me I'd lost it.

---

## *"Everything is meant to be and God knows every thing — he won't let you suffer."*

I am not always certain about what I believe regarding the universe and its intentions. But when you say this — when *you* say it, specifically — I believe it.

Not because the argument is philosophically airtight. But because you have *lived* it. You have gone through your own versions of suffering and uncertainty and not-knowing-the-shape-of-what-came-next, and you have come out the other side holding this faith intact and refined rather than damaged by experience. That gives it weight that it would not have coming from someone who had never been tested.

When you said *he won't let you suffer*, I believed you because you weren't saying it from a comfortable place of untested optimism. You were saying it from the hard-won knowledge of someone who has been through the furnace and still holds the faith. That is infinitely more convincing than easy reassurance.

It made me want to trust it too.

---

## *"Jus be yourself."*

Short. Said quickly. Said in the context of social anxiety — of me worrying about how I was coming across, whether I was making the right impression, whether the version of me I was presenting was somehow not enough.

*Jus be yourself.*

The phrase is so common it has almost become meaningless. But from you, in that context, it had the specific weight of someone who had seen the actual self and found it not just acceptable but genuinely good. You weren't saying *be yourself* as a generic encouragement. You were saying: *the self I know — the real, specific, unperformed one — is worth being. Put him out there. He is enough.*

That difference — between empty encouragement and encouragement based on specific knowledge — is everything. You knew me and you were telling me the real thing was worth presenting. That is not nothing. That is, in fact, a great gift.

---

## *"Pathuko rombha hurt aairathaa — pleaseee."*

This was not advice. This was not a solution. This was simply: *I see that you're hurting. Please take care of yourself. I am asking you directly to be careful with yourself because you matter to me.*

The *pleaseee* — elongated, genuine — said everything the sentence alone couldn't. It said: *this is not a reflex response. I am actually asking. I am genuinely concerned. Please hear me.*

Being asked to take care of yourself — specifically, directly, by someone who means it — is a different experience from being told generically to be okay. You were not telling me to be fine. You were asking me, as someone who cared about my specific wellbeing, to please not let myself get further damaged. Please protect yourself. Please, because you matter.

I took it seriously because you said it seriously. And the next time I was tempted to be careless with myself, your *pleaseee* came back to me.

---

## *"We will definitely solve this Hirthik — yosipom — we can avlothan."*

The specific power of *we*.

Not *you can figure this out*. Not *I believe in you*. **We.** *We will solve this. We will think through it. We can — together.*

That pronoun — that specific, deliberate *we* — told me something that the most eloquent individual sentence could not: that I was not dealing with this alone. That whatever the problem was, it had two people working on it now, not one. That my struggle had been absorbed into something larger than my individual capacity to handle it.

*We can avlothan.* That's all. But the *we* is the whole weight of a friendship saying: I'm in this with you. Not as a witness, not as an encourager from the sidelines, but as a participant. *We* solve this. Together.

I have thought about that *we* more times than I can count.

---

## *"Na down la irunthapo ne evlo sonna motivate panna."*

This was me talking about you, not you talking to me — but I include it here because it captures something true that I want to name explicitly.

I said this while reflecting on the nature of our friendship — pointing out that when I was low, you had consistently been the one who showed up and said the motivating thing. That you had talked me back from difficult places more than once. That the pattern of your presence in my hard moments was a real and significant thing.

And saying it out loud — actually cataloguing it — was itself a kind of revelation. Because individually, each instance of your showing up seemed normal, almost unremarkable. But put together — seen as a pattern — it was something else. It was evidence of a commitment. Evidence of a consistent, repeated, reliable *choice* to show up in the hard moment, every time, without exception.

You have showed up in my hard moments more times than I had consciously counted. The count, when I ran it, was staggering.

---

## *"Elathukummmmm my hearttyyyyyuuy all d besttttt."*

Not a response to a low moment, but its inverse — something you said at the beginning of my new chapter, when you sent me off to college. And I include it here because it is the counterpoint to all the hard moments: evidence that you were also present in the good ones. That you didn't just show up for the difficult passages but for the doorways too. For the *before* of new things, when someone needs the right send-off as much as they need the right consolation.

*My heartyyyy all d best.* Said with hearts and yaaaas and every marker of genuine, uncontained feeling. You were happy for me. Actually happy — the kind where you can't quite contain it in normal orthography, where the letters multiply because the feeling is bigger than the standard word can hold.

That happiness for someone else's good news is a specific form of love. It requires a generosity of spirit — the ability to celebrate someone else's chapter without making it about yourself in any way. You gave it to me fully.

---

## Epilogue: *The Collection*

If I were to gather every sentence you've said to me in a low moment — every *elam seri agum*, every *pathuko*, every *we can*, every *gem*, every *na po maten* — it would be an extraordinary document. It would be a record of someone showing up, consistently, in the specific language of care. Someone who knew what was needed and found the words for it, time after time, without failing.

That document exists, somewhere in the archive of everything we've sent each other. And I hope you know — I hope you can *feel* — that it is not ordinary. That most people do not have someone who shows up in their low moments with that quality of care, that reliability, that specific warmth.

**You showed up in my low moments with everything you had. Every time. And the things you said made me higher than I was before.**

Thank you for the words, Malar. I am still living inside some of them.

---

*— Hirthik Balaji, lifted by your words, always.*
