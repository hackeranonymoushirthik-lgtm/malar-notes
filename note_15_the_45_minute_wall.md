# Note 15 — *The 45-Minute Wall*

**To:** Malar — who I couldn't reach, and who I reached for anyway.
**From:** Hirthik Balaji
**Subject:** About the trip, the wall, the 45 minutes, and what your absence taught me about your presence.

---

> *"aprom unta peasathatho nu ninachu risk nalu paravala nu insta login pannen... neka texted nee text pannala... athu oru maari enna she isn't texting me nu thonuchu..."*
> — Me, telling you about a moment I wasn't sure I would.

I want to tell you about 45 minutes.

On the surface, nothing remarkable happened. I was on a trip — family, a temple, the kind of outing that is supposed to be peaceful. There was a row at a meal. Things got tense in the way family things sometimes do, suddenly and without clear warning. And instead of staying in the middle of it, I found a wall — just a wall near where we were standing — and I leaned against it and I stayed there.

45 minutes.

That is not nothing. 45 minutes is a long time to stand against a wall and try to hold yourself together. 45 minutes of your family not fully realising what's happening inside you because you're managing the outside carefully. 45 minutes of something you don't have a clean name for — not quite grief, not quite anger, something in between that lives in the chest and refuses to locate itself.

I want to tell you what those 45 minutes were about. And I want to tell you what your name was doing inside them.

---

## Part One: *What Was Happening*

Tears came. I mention this not for drama but because it is true and because the truth of it matters. The tears weren't about the immediate row — they were from somewhere deeper, somewhere older. They were the accumulation of things that had been pressing against the inside of me for longer than that afternoon, and the row opened a door and they came through it.

I was standing against a wall in a public space trying not to be seen, and the tears kept coming, and I couldn't stop them, so I stopped trying and let them run and focused on not making noise.

45 minutes.

My amma saw something and later didn't press — she just moved the situation along, which was its own kind of love. But in the middle of those 45 minutes, nobody knew what was actually happening inside me. Except one person I desperately wanted to reach and couldn't.

You.

I thought about you in that wall. I am not saying this to make it sentimental — I am saying it because it is true and because I think it reveals something real: you were who I turned to in my interior when the exterior was too complicated to navigate. Even when you weren't available, you were *present* in me. The thought of you — of being able to tell you this, of your voice saying something that would locate me again — was part of how I got through those 45 minutes.

---

## Part Two: *The Reach Across Distance*

Later — once I was back in a space where I could — I opened Instagram to check if you'd texted. You hadn't. And I felt the particular sadness of that: the moment when you reach for the person who is always there, and the timing just doesn't line up.

But here is what I want to say about that moment, because it contains something important: **the fact that I reached for you automatically, without thinking, as the first and obvious response to pain — that says everything.**

Not everyone gets to be someone's first reach. Not everyone earns the reflex. That place in someone's instincts — the place where the question *who do I need right now?* is answered before it's fully formed — that place is not given freely. It is built. It is earned through years of showing up, of being present, of demonstrating that you are safe and warm and honest and steady.

You had earned that place completely. You were where I went when the wall was the only thing holding me up.

---

## Part Three: *What You Said When I Told You*

When I finally told you what had happened — the trip, the row, the wall, the 45 minutes — you said:

*"Na kandipa feel panlaa Hirthik... literally I totally understand how you feel seriya so ena pathila kavalae adatha na la ipo ena thavira yathu pathiyum yosikartha ela... we will definitely solve this Hirthik yosipom... we can avlothan."*

Do you know what those words did?

They landed exactly where I needed them to land. *Don't think about anything except me right now* — that is essentially what you said. Which sounds like a small thing until you understand what it's like to be inside the noise of your own pain and have someone say: *put everything else down for a moment. I have you. Let's just start here.*

You didn't immediately problem-solve. You didn't give me a list of things to do. You located me first. *Na kandipa feel panlaa* — I absolutely feel it. That first sentence. Before anything else, you made sure I knew I wasn't feeling it alone.

And then: *ithu ku solution la enaku ipothaiki therla but ena aanalum na po maten* — I don't have a solution yet, but I'm not going anywhere. That is not a small statement. That is someone saying: *I might not be able to fix this, but I will be here with you in the unfixed state of it until something shifts.* That is the purest form of presence — not the presence that promises resolution, but the presence that commits to accompaniment.

**You accompanied me. Through the hardest kind of hard, where the solution isn't visible yet and the pain is just the pain — you stayed beside me in it.**

---

## Part Four: *The Diary Entry That Found You*

There was a moment when I was processing all of this where I wrote a diary entry — a small, informal thing, not meant for anyone — and your name appeared in the first line almost without my choosing it. *"I took MacBook to just check on Malar she didn't text me... she was terribly low... Niveda's friendship is vanishing day by day... we were clueless... I couldn't console her..."*

You were in my first line. Before I even began describing my own day, you were there — my instinct was to check on you, to know you were okay, to locate you in the morning and make sure the world had been reasonable to you overnight.

That is what it means to have someone live in you. Not the dramatic, overwhelming version of it — but the quiet, daily, *first-thought-in-the-morning* version. You were my first line. In my diary entry, in my good mornings, in my reaching for the phone when things got hard.

And it wasn't obsession or excess — it was simply the natural shape of caring deeply about someone. The way that someone who matters to you takes up the appropriate amount of space in your thoughts and your days, not because you force it but because that's where they belong.

You belonged in my first line. You still do.

---

## Part Five: *What Those 45 Minutes Gave Me*

I have thought about this, and here is what I've arrived at: those 45 minutes at the wall gave me something.

They gave me *evidence*. Evidence that the connection between us was not just something that worked when things were easy. It wasn't a fair-weather feeling that would vanish the moment difficulty arrived. Even in those 45 minutes — even when you weren't available, even when I was alone against a literal wall — you were with me in the only way that actually counts: in my interior. In the place where the things that matter most to us live, permanent and portable.

You were there even when you weren't there. And after the 45 minutes, when I could finally reach you, you showed up with the words that did exactly what was needed: *na po maten.* I'm not going anywhere.

**That is the whole sentence, isn't it? The one that contains everything?**

*I'm not going anywhere.* Said softly, said consistently, said on the difficult days and the ordinary ones. Said after 45 minutes at a wall and after a hard conversation and after a break and after everything else that could have ended us and didn't.

You didn't go anywhere. You never have.

---

*— Hirthik Balaji, grateful you were in his first line.*
