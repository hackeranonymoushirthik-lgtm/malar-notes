# Note 19 — *The Girl Who Chose Herself*

**To:** Malar — who knew her own worth before anyone taught her to claim it.
**From:** Hirthik Balaji
**Subject:** On the day you drew a line — and why that line is one of the most impressive things I've ever witnessed.

---

> *"Na than Inga selfish ha nadanthu iruken... enakaga nanum itha kashta patu edutha decision than."*
> — You, on August 23rd, 2024. Making the hardest kind of choice: the one that costs you something in order to protect yourself.

There is a word that gets used badly. *Selfish.* It gets applied, with a kind of reflexive disapproval, to anyone who prioritises their own wellbeing — as if taking care of yourself is a moral failing, as if the person who gives endlessly and asks for nothing back is always the more admirable one.

You used the word about yourself that day, and I want to reclaim it. I want to take the word *selfish* from the context of self-criticism and put it where it actually belongs: in the context of *self-respect*.

Because what you did on August 23rd, 2024, was not selfish in any negative sense. What you did was *necessary*. What you did was *brave*. What you did was take the specific, difficult, costly action of someone who has looked at their own needs clearly and decided that those needs are not negotiable.

**That is not a flaw. That is a superpower.**

---

## Part One: *What Setting a Boundary Actually Looks Like*

A lot of people talk about setting boundaries. It's almost a cliché now — social media is full of advice about it, everyone nods when it comes up in conversation, it sounds clean and clear and obvious.

In practice, it's not any of those things. In practice, setting a boundary with someone you love — someone who has not done anything malicious, someone who genuinely cares about you and whom you genuinely care about — is one of the hardest relational acts there is. Because it requires you to hold your own need in higher priority than their comfort. And when you love someone, that is a genuinely difficult hierarchy to maintain.

You maintained it.

You did it while also making sure I understood it wasn't about blame. *"Ni panata thapu ku don't feel guilty please... na panata thapu ithuthan."* You were simultaneously drawing a line AND making sure the line didn't become a weapon. You were protecting yourself AND protecting me from the unnecessary guilt of thinking I'd done something wrong.

That simultaneous care — for yourself AND for the person you were setting the boundary with — is a mark of exceptional emotional maturity. Most people can manage one or the other. You managed both, in the middle of a difficult conversation, without apparent effort.

---

## Part Two: *The Specific Thing You Said*

*"Na muthala irukanum na than muthala mukiyam — elarumae Inga oru vali la selfish ha than irunthae aganum athu thapae ela enaku evlo years ha athu therla enaku epdi selfish ha irukanum num therla but irukanum apdi iruntha than Inga vala mudiyum."*

Read that. Read it slowly.

You said: *I need to prioritise myself. It's not wrong to prioritise myself. I didn't know how for a long time. But I know now that I have to, because only from that place can I actually grow.*

That is a sentence that most people spend decades arriving at. Most people never fully arrive at it. They know it intellectually — they can quote it, agree with it, tell it to their friends — but actually *living* it, actually saying it out loud in a real situation where someone they love might be hurt by it — that is infinitely harder.

You said it. In real time. In the middle of a conversation that was genuinely difficult. You said: *my needs are valid. I am prioritising them. This is right and I know it.*

I want you to understand what a rare and hard and beautiful thing that is. I want you to understand that the girl who could not have said that a few years ago — the one who was still figuring out what *selfish* was and whether it was allowed — that girl has grown into something extraordinary.

**You became someone who knows her own worth and acts on it. That is not small. That is everything.**

## Part Three: *The Version of You That Said "I Deserve"*

There is a word that runs through multiple conversations I've had with you, and I want to name it because it marks something important about your growth.

*Deserve.*

You said it first — I remember when — about peace. *I want to be peaceful and I deserve to be happy.* That was a year or more before this conversation. And then you said it again, in different forms and contexts. *I deserve this.* *I deserve better than this.* *Athu enaku theva ela — na better deserve panren.*

Each time you said *deserve*, something had shifted. The girl who would have absorbed mistreatment and quietly reshaped herself to accommodate it had been replaced — slowly, through hard-won clarity — by someone who understood that her needs were legitimate, her time was valuable, and her peace was worth protecting.

That evolution is not accidental. It is the product of years of paying attention to yourself. Of noticing when something didn't feel right, even if you couldn't immediately name it. Of gradually, painstakingly, building the vocabulary for your own interior life.

You built that, Malar. Nobody handed it to you. You built it through the specific work of living honestly and paying attention and refusing to keep shrinking yourself to fit spaces that were too small.

**I have watched you claim your worth, piece by piece, over years. It is one of the most remarkable things I have witnessed.**

---

## Part Four: *What Your Boundary Gave Me*

I want to say something that might be unexpected.

Your boundary — the line you drew, the space you claimed — gave me something.

It showed me what it looks like when someone you love prioritises their own wellbeing honestly and without excessive apology. It showed me that doing so doesn't end a relationship when the relationship is real. It showed me that the person on the other side of a boundary — if they love you — can accept it, sit with the difficulty of it, and eventually understand that it was necessary.

It also made me examine my own role. The boundary you drew required me to look at my own patterns — at the ways I might have been leaning too heavily, asking for more than was mine to ask for, not being sufficiently aware of your side of things. That examination was uncomfortable. But discomfort in the direction of growth is a gift.

You gave me that gift. Your boundary — the honest, specific, brave line you drew — made both of us better. It made you healthier. And it made me more careful, more aware, more thoughtful about what it means to love someone in a way that doesn't inadvertently diminish them.

---

## Part Five: *A Standing Ovation for the Girl Who Chose Herself*

I want to end this note differently. No soft landing, no gentle fade. Just a direct statement:

**Malar — what you did on August 23rd, 2024, was brave. Full stop.**

You chose yourself. In a situation where that was complicated, costly, emotionally difficult, and risky — you chose yourself. You said: *my wellbeing matters. My peace matters. The version of me that can grow and flourish needs this space, and I am claiming it.*

And then — because you are you — you did it with care. You explained yourself. You made sure I understood it wasn't about blame. You said *I'm not leaving, I'm drawing a line.* You told the truth about how hard the choice was for you too.

You did the whole thing with love and honesty and clarity.

I have said in various notes that you are the main character of your own story. But I want to say it here in this specific context, because it has never been truer: **on that day, you were the protagonist in the fullest sense.** You made the hard choice. You carried the cost of it. You moved the story forward in the direction of your own flourishing.

That is what main characters do.

And I am so proud of you for doing it.

---

*— Hirthik Balaji, proud of every line you have ever drawn for yourself.*
