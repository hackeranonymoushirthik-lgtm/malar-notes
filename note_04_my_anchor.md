# Note 04 — *My Anchor*

**To:** Malar — the one who kept me from drifting, every single time.
**From:** Hirthik Balaji
**Subject:** About what it means to be someone's anchor — and why you have always been mine.

---

> *"Na down la irunthapo ne evlo sonna motivate panna — appo ella sadi thancu va?"*
> — Me, trying to tell you what you already knew but wouldn't fully claim.

I want to tell you about a particular kind of feeling. The feeling of floating. Not in the beautiful way — in the untethered way. The way where everything feels slightly too far away to touch, where your sense of direction dissolves, where you're going through the motions of your day but something fundamental is off-centre and you can't quite name what it is.

I have been in that place more than once. And more than once, you were the reason I found my way back.

---

## Part One: *What Drifting Looks Like*

I want to be specific here, because I think specificity is what makes a note like this honest rather than sentimental.

There were periods in my life — and we've talked through several of them, you and I, across hundreds of messages and countless late-night texts — where I was genuinely struggling. Struggling with my sense of direction. Struggling with my friend group, with the feeling that people I cared about were pulling apart and I was standing in the middle, trying to hold things together that were already breaking. Struggling with the kind of low-grade uncertainty that doesn't have a single clear source — it's just *there*, coloring everything grey.

In those times, I didn't always know how to ask for help. I didn't always have the language for what was wrong. I would send a message that was short where my messages weren't usually short. I would go quiet in ways that weren't quite like me. I would say "li8 ha" when I clearly didn't mean it.

You noticed. Every time, you noticed.

Not in a way that was intrusive or performative. You didn't make a big production of having noticed. You just... *asked*. You knocked on the door of whatever I was going through and said, in your way: *"enna achu? Solu."* And somehow that was always enough to make the door open.

---

## Part Two: *The Night I Needed You and You Were There*

There was a time — and I think you know which kind of time I mean, even if the specific one has blurred a little in memory — when I was genuinely unmoored. Friends were in conflict. I was exhausted. I had tried to fix things and failed. I was sitting in that particular kind of frustrated helplessness that comes from caring too much about something you can't control.

You talked me through it.

Not by solving it — you were wise enough to know that some things can't be solved, only survived. But by doing the thing you always do: *reorienting me toward what mattered.* You reminded me of my own capacity. You reminded me that my emotional exhaustion was a sign of how much I cared, not a flaw. You said something like *"always we will heal together"* — and you meant it for both of us, collectively, not just as a comfort extended in one direction.

*We will heal together.* Malar, that phrase lives in me. Because it captured something essential about what we are to each other: two people who have, in their different ways, needed healing, and who have chosen to be part of each other's healing rather than managing their wounds alone.

You made me feel like I didn't have to figure it out solo. You made me feel like someone was in my corner — not with easy answers but with real presence. And in the moments when I was most at sea, that presence was the anchor.

---

## Part Three: *How You Gave Stability Without Rigidity*

Here is something subtle and beautiful about the way you anchor people, Malar: **you don't try to make them into something fixed**. You don't anchor by telling people who to be or how to feel or what to want. You anchor by *being consistent yourself* — so that whoever they are at any given moment has somewhere stable to land.

You didn't need me to be okay to show up for me. You were willing to meet me exactly where I was — in the uncertainty, in the confusion, in the "na usurooda irukurathu pudikalaya" moments that I said half-jokingly but you heard completely seriously. You didn't flinch from those moments. You didn't recoil or try to redirect me toward cheerfulness before I was ready for it.

You just *were there.*

And that consistency — that reliability of your presence — is what made you an anchor. An anchor doesn't judge the ship for drifting. It doesn't tell the ship it should have drifted less or in a different direction. It just holds. It just stays. It just says: *here is something that won't move, here is something you can measure yourself against, here is something that will still be here when you've sorted yourself out.*

You were that for me. Immovably, warmly, un-showily — you held.

---

## Part Four: *Your Steadiness During My Noise*

One of the things I most want to acknowledge is this: **you were steady for me during periods when I was genuinely difficult to be steady for.**

When I was overthinking things — spiralling into the same concern for the fifth time — you didn't abandon me to the spiral but you also didn't let me live there indefinitely. You had this way of gently disrupting the pattern. You'd make me laugh when I needed to be cut loose from the seriousness. You'd say the practical thing when I was drowning in the theoretical. You'd remind me, with absolute zero condescension: *"vittattu papom enna nadakuthu nu."* Let it play out. See where it goes.

You had patience for my noise. Not infinite patience — you were human, and there were moments where you'd give me the equivalent of a gentle "okay but actually move on now" — but *generous* patience. Patient in the way that says: *"I know you're not like this always. I know this is the hard part. I'll stay with you until you come back to yourself."*

And I always came back to myself. Partly because I grew up and learned better coping strategies. But mostly — *mostly* — because I had you holding the space for the person I actually was when I wasn't lost in the fog.

---

## Part Five: *What It Means That You Were Never Loud About It*

The anchors that matter most in a life are often invisible in the way that all foundational things are invisible — you only notice them if they disappear.

You were never loud about being there for me. You didn't announce your constancy. You didn't catalogue your care or remind me how much you'd done or hold my difficult periods against me as debts I owed you. You just kept showing up. You kept being present. You kept checking in. And when I was in a better place, you were happy about it quietly — *genuinely* happy, without any of the competitive edge that lesser people bring to having supported someone through hard times.

That is the mark of someone who is there for the *right reasons*. Someone who cares about the outcome — your wellbeing — and not the recognition of their role in it. You never needed the credit. You just needed me to be okay.

**That is extraordinary. That is the kind of love that actually carries weight in the real world.**

---

## Epilogue: *Thank You for Holding*

I don't know if you've ever thought of yourself as someone's anchor. I suspect you haven't. I suspect you think you're just doing what any good friend does, being what any decent person tries to be.

But Malar — I want you to know that what you have been for me is specific and irreplaceable. There are people who show up for you when you're fine. There are people who celebrate with you when things are good. And then there are the rare, precious people who show up *in the drift* — who find you when you're lost and don't panic and don't leave and just stay, steady and present, until you find your way back.

You are in that rare category. You have always been in that rare category.

And every time I think about the version of my life without you in it — without your steadiness, without your warmth, without your honest voice in my ear reminding me that things work out and that I'm going to be okay — I genuinely can't picture it with any comfort.

**Because without my anchor, I would have drifted a lot further, and found my way back a lot harder.**

So: thank you. For holding. For staying. For never making it a thing that you stayed, which is somehow the most generous part of all.

You are the ground beneath my feet on the days when the ground felt like it was shaking.

---

*— Hirthik Balaji, anchored and grateful.*
