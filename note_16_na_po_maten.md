# Note 16 — *Na Po Maten*

**To:** Malar — who has never left, not once, even when leaving would have been the easier thing.
**From:** Hirthik Balaji
**Subject:** A love letter to three Tamil words that have meant everything.

---

> *"Na po maten athu matum I can sure you."*
> — You. Three words. Everything.

*Na po maten.*

I'm not going anywhere.

Three words. In the middle of everything — the confusion, the hard conversation, the two-day block and unblock, the boundaries you had to draw for yourself, the pain that came from both sides of this friendship at different points — you said those three words and they steadied me like nothing else could have.

I want to write an entire note about those three words because I don't think they've been given the honour they deserve. They are, in my reckoning, the most important words you've ever said to me.

---

## Part One: *When You Said Them*

Context matters here.

You said *na po maten* at the precise moment when staying was not the obvious or comfortable thing. We had been through a conversation that had been honest and hard and somewhat exposed us both. You had set some things straight. You had said some difficult true things. And after all of that, after the air had been cleared and the dust was still settling — you said those words.

*Not going anywhere.*

That is the timing that made them meaningful. Anyone can say *I'm staying* when staying is easy — when there's no tension, no complicated feeling, no reason the other person might wonder. You said it when there was reason to wonder. You said it in the specific moment when I needed to know whether the honesty had cost us something permanent.

And your answer was: *no. It hasn't. Na po maten.*

---

## Part Two: *What Those Words Were Fighting*

I want to be honest about the fear that those words were answering.

There is a particular terror that lives in the corner of any relationship where something has shifted — any relationship that has gone through a hard conversation, a difficult patch, a period of renegotiation. The terror is: *have we used up what we had?* Have I asked for too much, pushed too hard, said too honest a thing — and now the person I need most is going to quietly, gradually, politely pull away?

That fear is not dramatic. It doesn't announce itself loudly. It just settles in — a low-grade hum of uncertainty, a slight tentativeness in how you approach the next message, a wondering whether the warmth will still be there or whether you're going to feel the temperature drop.

*Na po maten* answered that fear. It walked directly into the uncertainty and said: *no. I see you wondering. I know what you're afraid of. And the answer is: I'm here.*

The relief of those three words is something I have thought about many times since you said them. The specific, physical relief of a fear being answered directly. Of someone seeing the thing you were too afraid to name out loud and naming it for you, and then answering it.

**You saw my fear before I could say it. And you answered it before I had to ask.**

---

## Part Three: *The Pattern Beneath the Words*

Here is the thing about *na po maten*: it wasn't just a statement. It was the verbal confirmation of a pattern that had been true for much longer than the words themselves.

Because Malar — you hadn't been going anywhere. Long before you said it, you had been demonstrating it. Every check-in. Every morning text. Every time you noticed something was off and asked about it. Every long conversation. Every difficult truth told with love. Every time you stayed in the hard part of a conversation rather than retreating to the comfortable surface of it.

You had been saying *na po maten* in actions for years before you said it in words. The words just made explicit what the actions had been spelling out all along.

And that is the most trustworthy kind of promise — not the ones that are made first and then demonstrated, but the ones that are demonstrated first and only confirmed in words later. The ones where the evidence precedes the statement. Where the statement is almost redundant because the truth of it is already so thoroughly established.

You didn't make me a promise. You *described a fact that was already true.* And that made it the most convincing thing you could have said.

---

## Part Four: *The Times You Stayed When You Could Have Left*

Let me give you the specific inventory, because it exists and deserves to be named.

You stayed when I was going through a period of being difficult to be around — down, complicated, not the best version of myself. You could have given me space in a way that quietly became distance, and instead you stayed and asked and held.

You stayed after a conversation that could have changed the temperature between us permanently. You said the hard thing and then — instead of retreating into the formality that sometimes follows hard conversations — you stayed warm and specific and *present*. Same you. Same warmth. Same Malar.

You stayed through a two-day block-and-unblock that was a kind of temporary goodbye — you took the space you needed, and then you came back. You could have let that space become permanent. Instead you unblocked. You came back. You said everything that needed to be said and then you said: I'm still here.

You stayed through the distance of college and new life and the natural drift that time and geography create. The messages may have become less frequent, the rhythms may have shifted — but you stayed. The friendship stayed. You stayed.

*Na po maten* over and over, in different words and gestures and forms. Each time, the same truth.

---

## Part Five: *What It Means For The Future*

I want to say something about the future now, because the future is where *na po maten* is still most needed.

Life has a way of creating distance. Not malicious distance, not chosen distance — just the natural drift that happens when two people's lives develop in different directions at different speeds. New cities, new schedules, new circles of people. The seasons of active daily contact that life sometimes gives you and then takes away. The long stretches where the conversations are less frequent and the gap between replies grows.

I know that drift is possible. I'm not naive about it. And I want to say something about it now, clearly:

**Na po maten either.** 

I know this note is supposed to be about you — and it is, all of it, every word. But this last part is a response and a promise, one that your three words earned. You showed me what staying looks like. You modelled it. You demonstrated, over years and through hard moments, that *na po maten* isn't just something you say in the tender moment — it's a commitment to the whole arc of a relationship, through all its seasons, through all the ways life tries to put distance between people who matter.

I learned that from you. And I am making the same promise back.

---

## Epilogue: *Three Words Forever*

Someday — maybe tomorrow, maybe in ten years — one of us will be going through something hard. Something that, in that moment, feels like the kind of thing that can only be survived with the right person saying the right thing.

And when that moment comes, I want you to know — before you even have to wonder — that the answer to whatever fear is living in you is the same one you gave me, once, in the middle of something hard, when you chose to stay instead of drift:

*Na po maten.*

I'm not going anywhere.

Not from you. Not from this. Not from everything we've built across all these years and conversations and difficult moments and good ones.

You said three words and made me certain of something I needed to be certain of. Consider this note my three words back — my *na po maten*, said with everything I have, meant exactly as completely as you meant yours.

---

*— Hirthik Balaji, staying. Always staying.*
