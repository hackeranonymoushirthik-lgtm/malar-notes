# Note 10 — *What Our Conversations Were Made Of*

**To:** Malar — who turned a chat window into one of the most meaningful spaces in my life.
**From:** Hirthik Balaji
**Subject:** An inventory of everything we talked about — and why it mattered more than either of us realised.

---

> *"Seri nama matter ku varuvom..."*
> — You, always finding a way to get to the real thing underneath the ordinary conversation.

If someone asked me to describe what we talked about, I would need a lot of time and still not do it justice. Because on the surface, the answer would seem almost boring. We talked about school. About friends. About films we loved and films we roasted mercilessly. About food — your food, my food, the food I wasn't making but should have been. About tuition schedules and exam pressure and the particular agony of a syllabus that refuses to shrink. About Tamil film releases and Vijay and whether a particular director deserved the benefit of the doubt for the dozenth time.

Surface level, it sounds ordinary. And that's precisely the point.

Because underneath all of that ordinary conversation — threaded through every film debate and every *"saptiya?"* and every mundane update about where we were and what we were doing — was something else entirely. Something *alive*. A quality of connection that turned the ordinary into something I will carry for the rest of my life.

This note is an attempt to name what that thing was.

---

## Part One: *The Permission to Be Unguarded*

The first thing our conversations gave me — and this took me a while to name, because it doesn't announce itself — was permission to be completely unguarded.

Not every relationship offers that. In fact, most don't. Most conversations — even with people you like, even with people you trust — have a degree of performance in them. You're presenting a version of yourself that's slightly more composed, slightly more articulate, slightly more together than you actually are. You're editing in real time, shaping the story of yourself into something that reads well.

With you, I stopped doing that almost immediately. I'm not sure when it happened exactly — it wasn't a decision, it was a gradual lowering of the guard until there was nothing left to guard. I talked to you like I talked to myself. The incomplete thoughts, the contradictions, the feelings I hadn't fully processed yet, the opinions I wasn't sure I'd endorse on further reflection — all of it went out as-is. Raw and unedited.

And you received it without making me feel foolish for it. You never weaponised my unguardedness against me. You never referenced something I'd said in a tender moment in a way that made me wish I hadn't said it. You held my honesty with care. And that holding made me more honest, which made you hold it more carefully, which made me more honest still — a loop of expanding trust that I don't think either of us planned but both of us somehow built together.

**The unguarded conversation is a rare and precious thing. We had it. Thousands of times. I will never take that for granted.**

---

## Part Two: *The Specific Texture of Talking to You*

Let me try to describe what it actually *felt* like, in the texture of a conversation, to talk to you.

It felt like the pacing was right. Not too fast, not artificially slowed. You responded when you had something to say and didn't force response when you didn't. You let silences be silences without filling them with noise. And when you did speak — when you came back with your thought — it was *measured*. I could feel that you had actually sat with what I said before replying. That you were taking it seriously enough to engage with properly.

It felt like humour and depth weren't in competition. We could go from completely dissolving over something ridiculous — some film's terrible dialogue, some friend's absurd decision, some observation about the specific texture of Tamil school life — straight into something real and heartfelt, and it didn't feel jarring. The gear change was smooth because both modes were genuine. You were funny *and* thoughtful, and neither cancelled out the other.

It felt like you were genuinely curious about me. Not in a therapist way, not in a way that felt like you were collecting information — but in the way that a person is curious when they care. When they want to understand not just what you're saying but *why* you're saying it, what it reveals about who you are, what it means for who you're becoming.

Being the object of that curiosity — being someone that someone *wants to understand* — is one of the most warming experiences there is. I hope you know I felt it. And I hope you know I brought the same curiosity back, because you are inexhaustibly interesting to try to understand.

---

## Part Three: *The Conversations About the Hard Things*

Some of the conversations I treasure most were the hardest ones.

Not because I enjoy difficulty — but because the conversations where we went to difficult places together were the ones where the trust was most visible. You don't go to the hard places with someone you don't trust. You don't open the drawer that has the real stuff in it unless you believe the person across from you can handle what's inside.

We opened a lot of drawers together.

You told me things about your past — about Arvind, about the specific shape of that heartbreak, about the years you spent carrying it quietly — that you hadn't told many people. And you told me with a trust that I felt the weight of, that I tried to be worthy of. I sat with those things. I held them carefully. I tried to understand, and when I didn't understand, I asked, and you answered honestly.

And I told you things. About my confusions, my doubts, my friendships that were straining, the places where I was struggling to be the person I wanted to be. And you sat with those things. You engaged with them with a seriousness that made me feel like they were worth being taken seriously.

Those conversations — the ones that happened at odd hours, that ran longer than either of us intended, that touched things neither of us had fully articulated before — those were the ones that built something real between us. Not the pleasant surface stuff (though that mattered too), but the *under the surface* stuff. The real texture of two people's actual lives.

**Those conversations are some of the most valuable things I have ever been part of.**

---

## Part Four: *The Laughter That Lived In Our Words*

I cannot write about our conversations without giving extensive, loving attention to the *comedy* of them. Because Malar — some of our exchanges have been *unreasonably* funny.

You have a gift for the perfectly timed observation. For the sentence that arrives exactly when the tension needs releasing. For the callback to something from three weeks ago that is now suddenly relevant and lands perfectly. For the Tamil-English hybrid of a sentence that only makes complete sense in the exact language and context of our friendship and is somehow precisely hilarious.

I have laughed in conversations with you in a way I don't laugh anywhere else. The specific frequency of our humour — the things we both find funny, the shared references, the in-jokes that have accumulated over years into a private language — is one of my most treasured possessions. Not metaphorically. *Actually* treasured.

The laughter was not decoration on top of the serious conversations. It was woven through everything. Even when we were talking about hard things, there would be a moment — a perfectly placed absurd observation, a sudden sideways take on something heavy — that would crack the gravity just enough to breathe. And in that breathing, we could go back to the hard stuff with more capacity. The laughter was structural. It held us up.

Your comedy is a gift, Malar. It is *specifically* your gift, the particular brand of it, the way it arrives and the way it lands. I want to name it properly because I don't think you always recognise it as the thing it is: *one of your most genuinely wonderful qualities.*

---

## Part Five: *The Conversations That Happened Without Words*

Some of the most significant moments in our communication were the ones where very little was said.

A short reply on a day when the reply would usually be long. A single emoji that said more than a paragraph. A change in tone that I could read because I had been paying attention long enough to know what was normal. The *"hmhm"* that meant you were processing and not yet ready to speak. The silence after a message that landed somewhere real, where neither of us immediately filled it because the feeling needed a moment to sit.

We learned each other's languages. Not just the words but the spaces between them. And that learning — that careful, patient, ongoing attention to the specific way the other person communicates — is itself a form of intimacy. It says: *I am paying close enough attention to read you. Not the public version, not the performed version — the actual you, in this actual moment.*

That kind of reading is rare. That kind of being read is rarer. We gave it to each other. And I am not sure we fully valued it while we were doing it because it became simply the texture of our communication — the water we swam in. But I value it now. I see it clearly now for what it was.

---

## Epilogue: *What We Built Together*

In the space of our conversations — thousands of them, across years, at every hour, covering everything from film releases to heartbreak to what's for dinner — we built something.

Not a structure you can point to or a project you can name. Something more like a *world*. A shared world with its own language and history and texture and ongoing story. A world where I knew I would be genuinely received, where honesty was the norm, where laughter was always available, where the hard things could be said and held.

That world matters to me more than I can put cleanly into words. It is one of the things in my life I would least want to lose. It is one of the things I am most grateful to have been built at all.

**You built it with me, Malar. Every message, every question, every honest answer, every laugh — all of it was a brick in something that will outlast the individual conversations. Something that has already changed who I am and will keep changing who I become.**

Thank you for talking to me. Thank you for having always meant it.

---

*— Hirthik Balaji, in the world we built.*
